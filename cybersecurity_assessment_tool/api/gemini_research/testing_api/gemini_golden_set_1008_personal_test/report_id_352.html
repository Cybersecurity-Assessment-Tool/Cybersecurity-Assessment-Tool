Executive Summary

This report details the cybersecurity posture of Titanium Core based on a review of organizational data, security questionnaire responses, and an external network scan. The assessment identified several critical gaps in foundational security controls, particularly concerning Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) implementation and comprehensive security awareness training. While the external network scan revealed no immediate exploitable open ports on the target, the policy and procedural weaknesses present significant internal risks that could lead to unauthorized access, data breaches, or operational disruption. Immediate recommendations focus on implementing robust MFA across key systems, developing essential security policies, and establishing recurring security training programs.

Risk Assessment

Based on the analysis of the security questionnaire and technical scan, the following risks and gaps have been identified. Severity levels are assigned based on potential impact and likelihood of exploitation.

Risk Name Overview Severity
Lack of MFA for Email Access Without Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email, accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks. Compromised email can lead to business email compromise (BEC), data loss, and unauthorized access to other linked systems. High
Lack of MFA for Computer Logins Without MFA for computer logins, internal systems are vulnerable to credential theft via malware, phishing, or physical access. This significantly increases the risk of lateral movement and privilege escalation within the network if a single set of credentials is compromised. High
Absence of Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) A lack of a formal AUP means employees may not be aware of their responsibilities regarding company IT resources, data handling, and internet usage. This can lead to increased legal liabilities, data breaches, and non-compliance with industry regulations due to employee misuse or negligence. Medium
Inadequate Annual Security Awareness Training While new employees receive training, the absence of annual refresher training for all employees leads to knowledge decay regarding evolving threats, best practices, and organizational policies. This increases the human element risk, making the organization more susceptible to phishing, social engineering, and malware attacks over time. Medium

Recommendations

To enhance the cybersecurity posture of Titanium Core, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access: Immediately deploy and enforce MFA for all email accounts. This critical control significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins: Enforce MFA for all user logins to company computers and endpoints. This adds a crucial layer of security against unauthorized access, even if primary credentials are stolen.

  3. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines employee responsibilities and expectations regarding the use of company IT resources, data, and internet access. Ensure all employees acknowledge and understand this policy.

  4. Establish Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees: Supplement new employee training with mandatory annual security awareness training for all staff. This ensures continuous education on current threats, organizational policies, and best practices, reinforcing a strong security culture.

  5. Regularly Review and Update Security Policies: Periodically review all security policies and procedures (including the AUP and training programs) to ensure they remain current with evolving threats and technological changes.
