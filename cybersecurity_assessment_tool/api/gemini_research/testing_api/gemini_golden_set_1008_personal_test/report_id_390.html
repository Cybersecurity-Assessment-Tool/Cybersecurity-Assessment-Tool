Executive Summary

This report details the cybersecurity posture of Titanium Core, based on a recent network scan, organizational security questionnaire responses, and an existing risk register. The analysis reveals a mixed security posture with notable strengths in internal access controls (MFA for computers and sensitive data) and employee security awareness training. However, critical gaps exist in fundamental areas such as Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email access and the formalization of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). A network scan of the target indicated that Port 80, often associated with unencrypted web services, was closed. This finding is critical as it potentially mitigates a previously identified risk, though further validation is recommended to confirm its current status. Immediate recommendations are provided to address the identified vulnerabilities and enhance the overall security stance.

Key Observations from Questionnaire

  • MFA for Email: The absence of mandatory MFA for email access is a significant vulnerability. Email accounts are frequently targeted by adversaries and can serve as a primary vector for credential theft and subsequent lateral movement or data exfiltration.

  • Employee Acceptable Use Policy: The lack of an established Acceptable Use Policy can lead to ambiguity regarding employee responsibilities and permissible use of organizational IT resources, increasing risks of misuse and non-compliance.

  • Positive Controls: The organization has positive controls in place for MFA on computers and sensitive data systems, and a commendable commitment to regular security awareness training. These are strong foundational elements that should be maintained and potentially expanded.

Recommendations

To enhance the cybersecurity posture of Titanium Core, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Mandatory Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Action: Immediately implement and enforce MFA for all employee and administrative email accounts across the organization.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise, credential stuffing attacks, and unauthorized access to linked services. This is a critical control for modern cybersecurity.

  2. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a comprehensive AUP outlining the proper and permissible use of organizational IT assets, data, and networks. Ensure all employees are trained on the AUP, and obtain formal acknowledgment of understanding and compliance.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear guidelines for employee behavior, reduces misuse of resources, improves compliance with legal and regulatory requirements, and provides a clear basis for addressing policy violations.

  3. Review and Validate Pre-existing Risk: Unencrypted Web Server:

    • Action: Investigate the discrepancy between the pre-existing risk statement ("Port 80 is open") and the current network scan results ("Port 80: Closed" on ). Confirm whether the risk has been fully mitigated, applies to a different host/service not covered by this scan, or if the risk register entry is outdated. Update the risk register accordingly.

    • Benefit: Ensures the accuracy and relevance of the risk register, allowing for proper prioritization of resources on currently active and significant threats. If the risk is still active on other assets, appropriate remediation (e.g., implementing HTTPS/SSL certificates) can be pursued.

  4. Continue and Expand Security Awareness Training:

    • Action: Leverage the existing security awareness training program to specifically educate employees on the importance of MFA (especially for email) and the new AUP (once implemented). Include updated training modules on identifying advanced phishing attempts and social engineering tactics.

    • Benefit: Reinforces secure behaviors, raises overall security consciousness, and empowers employees to become an effective first line of defense against cyber threats.
