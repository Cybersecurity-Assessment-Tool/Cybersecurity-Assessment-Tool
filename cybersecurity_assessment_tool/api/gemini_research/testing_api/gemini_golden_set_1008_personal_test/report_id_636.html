Executive Summary

This report details the cybersecurity posture assessment for New Era. The analysis is based on organizational security questionnaire responses, with an attempt to integrate network scan data and pre-existing vulnerability information.

The organization demonstrates a strong commitment to foundational security controls by implementing Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) across email, computer logins, and sensitive data systems, alongside consistent employee security awareness training for both new hires and existing staff. This proactive stance significantly mitigates common access-related risks.

However, a critical gap was identified regarding the absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). This omission can lead to inconsistent employee behavior, increased risk of data misuse, and potential compliance issues. Furthermore, a comprehensive technical posture assessment was hampered due to malformed or unavailable network scan data (Input_1_Network_Scan_JSON) and pre-existing vulnerability data (Input_3_Current_Risks_JSON).

Immediate recommendations include the development and implementation of a robust AUP, along with ensuring proper and complete data collection for future technical and risk assessments to enable a holistic security review.

Recommendations

The following actionable recommendations are provided to improve the cybersecurity posture of New Era, addressing identified gaps and limitations in the assessment data.

  1. Implement a Comprehensive Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Develop and formally implement a clear, concise, and comprehensive AUP that explicitly outlines employee responsibilities for using all company IT resources, proper data handling procedures, acceptable internet and email usage, and adherence to security protocols.

    • Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge the AUP, with mechanisms for regular review and re-affirmation (e.g., annually or upon significant policy changes).

    • Integrate AUP review and understanding into both new employee onboarding and annual security awareness training programs.

  2. Conduct a Robust Network Scan and Vulnerability Assessment:

    • Engage a qualified third party or utilize in-house tools to perform a comprehensive and authenticated network scan. This scan should accurately identify all open ports, running services, software versions, and potential misconfigurations on both external and internal facing systems.

    • Prioritize the remediation of identified vulnerabilities (e.g., patching outdated software, reconfiguring insecure services, removing unnecessary access) based on their severity and potential impact.

    • Regularly schedule such scans (e.g., quarterly) to maintain an up-to-date view of the organization’s technical attack surface.

  3. Establish and Maintain a Vulnerability Management Program:

    • Implement a formal process for identifying, assessing, tracking, and remediating vulnerabilities across the organization’s assets.

    • Ensure that an accurate and up-to-date inventory of known vulnerabilities and risks within the organization is maintained.

    • Implement systems to ensure this vulnerability data is consistently collected, stored in a usable format, and accessible for future security assessments, enabling comprehensive risk correlation and informed decision-making.

  4. Continue and Enhance Strong MFA and Security Awareness Training:

    • Maintain the current excellent practice of enforcing Multi-Factor Authentication for all access points identified (email, computer logins, sensitive data systems) and explore opportunities to extend MFA to other critical systems where applicable.

    • Ensure security awareness training programs remain current, engaging, and relevant to emerging threats, continuing to be delivered consistently to both new hires and existing employees at least once per year.
