Risk Assessment

Based on the analysis of the provided data, the following high-priority risks have been identified primarily from the security questionnaire responses. No technical vulnerabilities were found via the provided scan, and no existing vulnerabilities were provided for review.

Risk Name Overview Severity
Lack of Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) Without a formal AUP, employees may lack clear guidelines regarding the appropriate use of organizational IT assets, data, and internet resources. This can lead to misuse, inadvertent data breaches, and non-compliance with regulatory requirements. High
Insufficient Annual Security Awareness Training While new employees receive training, the absence of mandatory annual security awareness training for all employees means that critical security knowledge may become outdated or forgotten. This significantly increases the organization’s susceptibility to human error-related incidents, such as phishing attacks and social engineering. High

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and strengthen the overall cybersecurity posture, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Develop and Implement a Comprehensive Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Draft and formalize a clear, concise AUP that defines acceptable and unacceptable use of all organizational IT resources, including hardware, software, network access, and data.

    • Ensure all employees formally acknowledge and sign the AUP upon hiring and annually thereafter.

    • Integrate AUP principles into all employee onboarding and security awareness training programs.

  2. Establish Mandatory Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees:

    • Implement a recurring, mandatory security awareness training program for all employees (not just new hires) at least once per year.

    • Training content should be engaging, relevant to current threats (e.g., advanced phishing, ransomware), and cover key topics such as data protection, incident reporting procedures, and the importance of adhering to the AUP.

    • Track completion rates and conduct periodic assessments (e.g., simulated phishing campaigns) to gauge effectiveness and identify areas for improvement.

  3. Maintain and Enhance Proactive Network Hardening Measures:

    • Continue to enforce strict firewall rules and implement network segmentation to minimize the attack surface and contain potential breaches.

    • Regularly review and update network configurations, and consider periodic vulnerability assessments and penetration testing on all critical systems, including both external and internal assets, to proactively identify and remediate weaknesses.

Conclusion

Great Lakes has demonstrated a commendable commitment to implementing Multi-Factor Authentication, which is a critical control against credential theft. However, the identified deficiencies in establishing a formal Acceptable Use Policy and providing continuous, organization-wide security awareness training represent significant human and governance-related risks. Addressing these foundational elements is paramount to fostering a robust security culture and significantly enhancing the organization’s overall resilience against the evolving landscape of cyber threats. A commitment to continuous improvement across all facets of cybersecurity will be key to long-term protection.
