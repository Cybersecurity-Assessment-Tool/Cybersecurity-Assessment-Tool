3.1. Key Gaps Identified

Based on the questionnaire, the following critical gaps were identified:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access: Email is a primary vector for phishing and credential compromise. The absence of MFA significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access to email accounts, potentially leading to business email compromise (BEC) or broader network intrusion.

  • Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): An AUP sets clear expectations for employee behavior regarding company IT resources. Without one, employees may unknowingly engage in risky activities, and the organization lacks a clear framework for enforcement and accountability.

5.2. Detailed Risk Description: Localhost Exposed

The existing risk data explicitly identifies a "Localhost Exposed" vulnerability with a severity CVSS score of 10.0, classified as Critical. This risk is directly corroborated by the network scan showing 127.0.0.1 as an active host with port 22 (SSH) open. While 127.0.0.1 is typically an internal loopback address, its presence in a "Localhost Exposed" risk suggests that an internal service meant to be isolated might be accessible or misconfigured in a way that poses a threat. The open SSH port exacerbates this, providing a potential remote access vector, which could be exploited if the internal service is misconfigured or vulnerable.

6. Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and improve the overall cybersecurity posture, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Action: Immediately deploy and enforce MFA for all email accounts across the organization. Prioritize user education on its importance and usage.

    • Rationale: This is a critical defense against phishing, credential stuffing, and other forms of unauthorized access, significantly reducing the risk of email account compromise.

  2. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a clear, comprehensive AUP for all employees regarding the use of company IT resources, equipment, and networks. Ensure it is formally communicated, understood, and signed by all employees, including new hires.

    • Rationale: An AUP establishes clear guidelines for acceptable behavior, minimizes risky employee actions, and provides a formal framework for enforcement and accountability.

  3. Investigate and Secure "Localhost Exposed" Vulnerability:

    • Action: Conduct an in-depth investigation into why 127.0.0.1 is considered "Exposed" with a CVSS 10.0 risk, especially concerning the open SSH port 22. Determine if the exposed service is intentional, and if so, what security measures are in place. If unintentional, identify the cause and remediate the exposure.

    • Rationale: A critical vulnerability on localhost suggests a severe misconfiguration or an internal service being inadvertently accessible, posing a significant threat to internal systems if compromised.

  4. Harden SSH Service on 127.0.0.1:

    • Action: If SSH access on 127.0.0.1 is legitimate and required, ensure it is configured with strong security practices. This includes disabling password authentication in favor of key-based authentication, disallowing root login, implementing strong access controls (e.g., firewall rules to limit source IPs), and ensuring only necessary users have access. Consider changing the default port.

    • Rationale: SSH, if not properly secured, can be a target for brute-force attacks and unauthorized access, particularly when combined with an "Exposed" status.

  5. Regular Security Awareness Training (Refresher):

    • Action: While current training is conducted, reinforce the importance of security policies, including MFA usage, password hygiene, and responsible use of company assets, through regular, engaging, and updated security awareness training.

    • Rationale: Continuous training helps maintain a strong security culture and keeps employees updated on evolving threats and best practices.
