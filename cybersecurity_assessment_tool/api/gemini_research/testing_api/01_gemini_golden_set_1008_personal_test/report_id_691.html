Analysis of Control Gaps

Two critical control gaps were identified:

  • No MFA for Email: Email is a primary vector for phishing, business email compromise (BEC), and account takeover attacks. The absence of MFA on email accounts is a critical vulnerability.

  • No Security Training for New Employees: New hires are often targeted by attackers. Failing to provide immediate security awareness training leaves a window of high vulnerability until the annual training cycle.

Analysis of Technical Findings

The scan identified an open SSH port (22). While SSH is a secure protocol, its exposure to the public internet creates a significant attack surface. It can be targeted by brute-force password attacks, credential stuffing, and exploits against vulnerable versions. Further investigation is required to determine the specific SSH server version and its configuration.

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and improve the overall security posture, the following actions are recommended with urgency.

  1. Implement MFA for Email (Priority: Immediate):

    • Action: Enforce mandatory Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all user and administrative email accounts immediately.

    • Justification: This is the single most effective control to prevent email account takeovers and will significantly reduce the risk of phishing and Business Email Compromise (BEC) attacks.

  2. Integrate Security Training into Onboarding (Priority: High):

    • Action: Develop and integrate a mandatory security awareness training module into the new employee onboarding process. This should be completed within the first week of employment.

    • Justification: This closes the vulnerability window for new hires and ensures a baseline security awareness level across the entire organization from day one.

  3. Secure the Exposed SSH Service (Priority: High):

    • Action:

      1. Determine the business justification for the publicly exposed SSH service.

      2. If not required, disable access from the internet immediately.

      3. If required, implement compensating controls: restrict access to known, authorized IP addresses via firewall rules, enforce public key authentication (disabling passwords), and ensure the SSH server software is fully patched.

    • Justification: This reduces the external attack surface and protects a key potential entry point into the organization’s network.
