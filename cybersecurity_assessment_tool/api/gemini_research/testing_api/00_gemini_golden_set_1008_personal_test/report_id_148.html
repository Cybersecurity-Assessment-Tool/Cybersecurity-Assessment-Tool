Analysis of Questionnaire Responses

The questionnaire highlights several critical areas for improvement:

  • MFA for Email Access: The lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email access is a significant vulnerability. Email is a primary vector for phishing and account compromise, and without MFA, a stolen password can grant attackers full access.

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of an AUP leaves employees without clear guidelines on the appropriate use of organizational IT resources. This can lead to increased risks from unintentional misuse or malicious activities, as expectations are not formally set.

  • Security Awareness Training for New Employees: Failing to provide security awareness training to new employees from the outset means that new hires may unknowingly introduce risks or fall victim to social engineering attacks during their critical onboarding period.

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

Immediate Actions (High Priority)

  1. Implement MFA for Email Access:

    • Action: Immediately enable and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all organizational email accounts.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise, even if passwords are stolen, by requiring a second verification factor.

  2. Develop and Implement New Employee Security Awareness Training:

    • Action: Create a mandatory security awareness training program for all new employees to be completed during onboarding.

    • Benefit: Equips new hires with essential knowledge to recognize and respond to common cyber threats (e.g., phishing, malware) from day one, reducing the likelihood of human error leading to breaches.

Mid-Term Actions (Medium Priority)

  1. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Draft, approve, and disseminate a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines expectations for employee conduct regarding IT resource usage, data handling, and internet browsing. Obtain signed acknowledgments from all employees.

    • Benefit: Provides clear guidelines, reduces misuse of company resources, enhances data protection, and establishes a foundation for disciplinary action in case of policy violations.

Ongoing Actions

  1. Maintain Annual Security Awareness Training:

    • Action: Continue with the current practice of providing annual security awareness training for all employees, ensuring content is updated regularly to reflect current threat landscapes.

    • Benefit: Reinforces security best practices, keeps employees informed about evolving threats, and fosters a strong security culture.

  2. Regular Network Scanning and Vulnerability Management:

    • Action: Periodically perform internal and external network scans and vulnerability assessments across the entire IT infrastructure (not just single targets) to identify and remediate potential weaknesses.

    • Benefit: Proactively identifies outdated software, misconfigurations, and other technical vulnerabilities before they can be exploited by attackers.

Conclusion

The assessment reveals a mixed security posture. While the network scan of the specific target showed no immediate external vulnerabilities, the organizational questionnaire identified significant gaps in fundamental security controls related to multi-factor authentication for email, acceptable use policies, and new employee security training. Addressing these policy and human-factor related risks should be the immediate priority for Deep Root Ecology to significantly enhance its overall cybersecurity resilience.
