Executive Summary

This report details the findings from a cybersecurity posture assessment for Opal Sky Media. The assessment leveraged organizational data, security questionnaire responses, and technical network scan results to identify strengths, weaknesses, and potential risks.

Overall, Opal Sky Media demonstrates a commendable commitment to security in certain areas, such as requiring Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email and sensitive data systems, and conducting annual security awareness training for all employees. However, significant gaps were identified that pose elevated risks to the organization’s security posture. Critical findings include the lack of MFA for computer logins, the absence of an employee acceptable use policy, and a failure to provide security awareness training for new employees. Furthermore, a technical scan revealed an exposed SSH service (port 22), which, while common, represents a potential attack vector if not rigorously secured.

Addressing these critical gaps and implementing the recommended measures will significantly enhance Opal Sky Media’s defense against common cyber threats and strengthen its overall security posture.

Analysis of Questionnaire Gaps

The questionnaire revealed three critical areas requiring immediate attention:

  1. Lack of MFA for Computer Logins: Not requiring MFA for computer logins significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access if employee credentials are stolen or compromised.

  2. Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Without an AUP, employees may not have clear guidelines on the appropriate use of company IT resources, potentially leading to security incidents, data leakage, or compliance issues.

  3. No Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New hires are often targets for social engineering attacks and may not be familiar with organizational security policies or common threats. The absence of initial training leaves them vulnerable.

Discussion of Risks

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Logins: This is a critical vulnerability. Even with strong passwords, without a second factor, a single credential compromise can lead to significant unauthorized access to internal systems, data, and applications.

  • Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): An AUP sets the foundational expectations for employee behavior regarding IT assets and data. Its absence creates ambiguity, increases the likelihood of accidental or malicious misuse, and complicates incident response and disciplinary actions.

  • No Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New employees are typically less familiar with an organization’s specific security policies and the common threats it faces. Failing to provide initial training leaves them as potentially easy targets for phishing, social engineering, and other attacks that could bypass technical controls.

  • Exposed SSH Service (Port 22): While SSH is a secure protocol, its exposure on the internet creates a visible target. Without additional details from the scan, it’s impossible to confirm specific vulnerabilities, but weak configurations (e.g., password authentication enabled, easy-to-guess passwords, root login permitted) could lead to unauthorized remote access.

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and enhance Opal Sky Media’s cybersecurity posture, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins:

    • Action: Mandate and deploy MFA for all employee computer logins, integrating with existing identity management systems where possible.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access due to compromised credentials.

  2. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Draft a comprehensive AUP outlining appropriate use of company IT resources, internet, email, and data. Disseminate it to all employees and obtain signed acknowledgments.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear expectations, reduces insider threats, and provides a framework for addressing misuse.

  3. Initiate Security Awareness Training for New Hires:

    • Action: Integrate mandatory security awareness training into the onboarding process for all new employees. This training should cover common threats (e.g., phishing), company policies, and best practices.

    • Benefit: Equips new employees with essential security knowledge from day one, reducing their susceptibility to social engineering.

  4. Strengthen SSH Security on 2001:db8::1:

    • Action: Review the configuration of the SSH service on 2001:db8::1. Ensure that password authentication is disabled in favor of key-based authentication, root login is disallowed, and consider implementing IP-based access restrictions (e.g., allow SSH only from trusted administrative networks).

    • Benefit: Reduces the attack surface and prevents brute-force or dictionary attacks against the SSH service.

  5. Conduct More Comprehensive Technical Scans:

    • Action: Perform regular, in-depth vulnerability scans, including authenticated scans, to identify specific service versions, configurations, and potential vulnerabilities on all external and internal systems.

    • Benefit: Provides a clearer picture of the technical attack surface and allows for proactive patching and configuration hardening.
