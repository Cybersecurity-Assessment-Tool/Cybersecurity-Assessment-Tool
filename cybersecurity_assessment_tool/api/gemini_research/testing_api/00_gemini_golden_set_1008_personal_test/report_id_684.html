3.1 Analysis of Control Gaps

The "No" responses highlight significant security control gaps:

  • MFA for Email Access: The absence of MFA for email access is a severe vulnerability. Email is a primary vector for phishing, business email compromise (BEC), and account takeover attacks. Without MFA, compromised credentials can grant attackers full access to communication channels, potentially leading to data breaches and financial fraud.

  • MFA for Computer Login: Not requiring MFA for computer login significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access to internal systems. Stolen or weak passwords can allow attackers to gain a foothold within the network, escalate privileges, and launch further attacks.

  • Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The lack of an AUP leaves the organization vulnerable to misuse of company resources, compliance violations, and legal liabilities. An AUP sets clear expectations for employee conduct regarding company assets and information, reducing the risk of accidental or malicious security incidents.

6. Recommendations

Based on the identified risks and control gaps, the following recommendations are provided to enhance Astraeus Aerospace’s cybersecurity posture:

  1. Implement MFA for Email Access (Critical):

    • Immediately enable and enforce MFA for all employee email accounts. Prioritize administrative and executive accounts.

    • Educate users on the importance of MFA and how to use it effectively.

  2. Implement MFA for Computer Login (Critical):

    • Roll out MFA for all computer logins, especially for devices accessing sensitive information or critical network segments.

    • Explore solutions compatible with existing directory services (e.g., Active Directory) and operating systems.

  3. Develop and Enforce an Employee Acceptable Use Policy:

    • Draft a comprehensive AUP that clearly defines appropriate use of company IT resources, data handling, internet usage, and device policies.

    • Ensure all employees review and sign the AUP, and provide regular training on its contents.

  4. Conduct a Comprehensive Technical Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Test:

    • Given the unavailability of previous scan data, it is crucial to perform a thorough, external, and internal vulnerability scan and a penetration test.

    • This should identify active open ports, misconfigured services, outdated software versions, and potential entry points for attackers.

  5. Establish a Robust Vulnerability Management Program:

    • Implement tools and processes for continuous vulnerability scanning and management.

    • Define clear procedures for identifying, triaging, patching, and verifying remediation of vulnerabilities.

    • Maintain an up-to-date inventory of all assets and their associated risks.

  6. Regular Security Awareness Training (Reinforce):

    • While currently performed, continuously update and enhance security awareness training to cover emerging threats, especially phishing and social engineering techniques.

    • Conduct simulated phishing campaigns to test employee resilience.

These recommendations, if implemented diligently, will significantly mitigate the identified risks and strengthen Astraeus Aerospace’s overall cybersecurity posture.
