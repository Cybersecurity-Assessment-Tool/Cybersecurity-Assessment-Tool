5. Risk Assessment

This section synthesizes the findings from the security control review and technical analysis, categorizing identified risks.

5.1. Identified High-Level Risks (from Questionnaire)

Based on the security control review, the following critical risks were identified:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access (Critical): Without Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email, accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks. A compromised email account can serve as a pivot point for broader network intrusion, data exfiltration, and business email compromise (BEC) scams.

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Login (Critical): Similar to email, not requiring MFA for logging into computers significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access if credentials are stolen or guessed. This can lead to direct access to internal systems, data, and critical infrastructure.

  • Inadequate Annual Security Awareness Training (High): While training for new employees is provided, the absence of regular, annual security awareness training for all employees leaves the organization vulnerable to evolving social engineering tactics, phishing attacks, and insider threats. Employees are the first line of defense, and their knowledge needs to be continuously updated.

6. Recommendations

Based on the findings, the following recommendations are provided to enhance the cybersecurity posture of Harbor Light Foundation.

  • Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Action: Immediately deploy and enforce MFA for all employee email accounts. This should be a mandatory requirement for accessing email services.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise, even if passwords are stolen.

  • Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Login:

    • Action: Implement MFA for logging into all company computers and workstations, especially those accessing sensitive data or critical systems.

    • Benefit: Adds a crucial layer of security, preventing unauthorized access to endpoints and the internal network.

  • Establish Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees:

    • Action: Develop and implement a mandatory annual security awareness training program for all employees, regardless of tenure. This training should cover current threats such as phishing, social engineering, ransomware, and proper data handling.

    • Benefit: Empowers employees to recognize and report threats, fostering a security-conscious culture and reducing the likelihood of human-factor incidents.

  • Consider Broader Internal Network Scanning Scope:

    • Action: While the single host scan showed no open ports, consider expanding internal network scans to cover a wider range of IP addresses and services within the internal network to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the attack surface.

    • Benefit: Provides a clearer picture of potential vulnerabilities on other internal systems and ensures no critical services are inadvertently exposed.

7. Conclusion

Harbor Light Foundation demonstrates a foundational commitment to security by having an acceptable use policy and initial security awareness training. However, the absence of MFA for email and computer logins, coupled with the lack of ongoing security awareness training, represents significant vulnerabilities. Addressing these critical control gaps should be the organization’s highest priority. By implementing the recommended actions, Harbor Light Foundation can substantially strengthen its defenses against common cyber threats and enhance its overall cybersecurity posture.
