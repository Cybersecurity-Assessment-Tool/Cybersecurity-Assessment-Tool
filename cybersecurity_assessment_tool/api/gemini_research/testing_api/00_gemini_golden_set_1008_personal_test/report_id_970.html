Executive Summary

This report provides an assessment of Swift Current Labs’s cybersecurity posture based on a security questionnaire and a perimeter network scan. The organization demonstrates some foundational security practices, such as Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for computer login and sensitive data systems, and initial security awareness training for new employees. However, critical gaps were identified in key areas, including the absence of MFA for email access, the lack of a formal employee acceptable use policy, and insufficient annual security awareness training for all employees. The perimeter scan did not identify any immediate external vulnerabilities or open services on the scanned target, but this should not be taken as a complete security guarantee without further internal and external testing. Addressing the identified control deficiencies is crucial to enhance the overall security posture and reduce organizational risk.

Identified Risks

The primary risks are derived from the "No" answers in the security control review. These represent significant weaknesses in the organization’s security posture.

Identified Risks and Severity
Risk Name Overview Severity
Lack of MFA for Email Access Email is a primary vector for phishing and account compromise. Without MFA, a compromised password could grant an attacker full access, leading to data breaches, business email compromise, and further attacks. High
Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) Without an AUP, employees may be unaware of their responsibilities regarding company IT resources, leading to misuse, data leakage, or the introduction of malware. This lack of formal guidance increases operational and security risks. Medium

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and strengthen Swift Current Labs’s cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement MFA for Email Access: Immediately deploy and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all user accounts accessing company email services. This significantly raises the bar for attackers attempting to gain unauthorized access, even if passwords are compromised through phishing or other means.

  2. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a clear and comprehensive AUP that outlines employee responsibilities for using company IT assets, data, and networks. Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge this policy. Integrate it into new hire onboarding processes and review it annually.

  3. Establish Annual All-Employee Security Awareness Training: Conduct mandatory, annual security awareness training for all employees, not just new hires. This training should be updated regularly to cover current threats (e.g., phishing, social engineering, ransomware), company policies, data handling best practices, and incident reporting procedures. Regular refreshers are crucial for maintaining a strong security culture and keeping employees informed.

  4. Conduct Comprehensive Vulnerability Assessments (External and Internal): While the initial perimeter scan yielded no findings (due to an empty target), it is strongly recommended to perform regular, authenticated vulnerability scans and penetration tests. These should cover both external (with a valid target IP) and internal networks, as well as critical applications, to identify potential weaknesses in systems, software, and network configurations before they can be exploited by malicious actors.
