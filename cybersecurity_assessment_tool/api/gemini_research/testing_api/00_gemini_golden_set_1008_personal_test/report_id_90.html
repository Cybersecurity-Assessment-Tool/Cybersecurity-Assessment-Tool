Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive cybersecurity posture assessment for Signal Flare, combining insights from organizational security practices, a targeted network scan, and existing risk data.

Signal Flare demonstrates a strong commitment to multi-factor authentication (MFA) across critical access points, including email, computer logins, and sensitive data systems. This significantly enhances protection against unauthorized access.

However, key gaps in foundational security practices were identified: the absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) and the lack of security awareness training for new employees pose substantial risks, potentially leading to policy violations, data misuse, and susceptibility to social engineering attacks from the outset of employment.

A technical scan of the internal target 192.168.0.5 revealed no open ports, indicating a secure configuration for this specific host at the time of the scan. A pre-existing risk regarding an unencrypted web server (Port 80 open) was noted in organizational records; however, this specific condition was not confirmed on the scanned host during this assessment, suggesting it might apply to a different asset or has been remediated.

Recommendations focus on establishing critical policies and training programs, alongside validating existing risk information.

Control Gaps Identified

Based on the questionnaire, two significant control gaps were identified:

  • Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Without a formal AUP, employees may be unaware of the proper and prohibited uses of organizational IT resources, data, and communication systems. This can lead to compliance violations, data exfiltration, or introduction of malware.

  • Lack of New Employee Security Awareness Training: While annual training for all employees is conducted, the absence of initial security awareness training for new hires means that new staff members might operate without fundamental security knowledge from their first day, making them vulnerable targets for social engineering, phishing, and other common attacks.

Recommendations

To strengthen Signal Flare’s cybersecurity posture, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

Immediate Actions

  • Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Draft a clear, comprehensive AUP that defines acceptable and unacceptable use of company IT assets, networks, and data.

    • Obtain legal review and management approval.

    • Disseminate the AUP to all employees, requiring acknowledgment of understanding and compliance.

    • Integrate AUP review into annual employee training.

  • Implement Mandatory New Employee Security Awareness Training:

    • Develop a foundational security awareness training module specifically for new hires.

    • Make completion of this training a mandatory part of the onboarding process, prior to granting access to critical systems.

    • Cover topics such as phishing, password hygiene, data handling, and reporting suspicious activities.

  • Validate "Unencrypted Web Server" Risk:

    • Review the scope and applicability of the existing "Unencrypted Web Server" risk (severity 5.0).

    • Confirm whether this risk applies to a different asset within the organization, or if it was remediated on since the risk was first identified.

    • If applicable to other assets, prioritize remediation efforts such as implementing HTTPS/SSL certificates.

Long-Term Strategy

  • Regular Policy Review and Updates: Ensure that all security policies, including the AUP, are reviewed and updated annually or whenever significant changes in technology, threats, or regulations occur.

  • Continuous Security Awareness Program Enhancement: Beyond initial and annual training, consider implementing regular security bulletins, phishing simulations, and targeted micro-trainings to keep security top-of-mind for all employees.

  • Periodic Internal Network Scans: Conduct regular internal network scans across various segments to proactively identify potential vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, or unauthorized services before they can be exploited.
