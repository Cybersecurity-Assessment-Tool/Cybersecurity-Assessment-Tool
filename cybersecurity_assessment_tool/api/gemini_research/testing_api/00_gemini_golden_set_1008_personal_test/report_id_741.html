Analysis of Security Controls

True Grit demonstrates good practices in requiring Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for computer logins and sensitive data systems, and maintains regular security awareness training. However, critical gaps exist: the absence of MFA for email access significantly increases the risk of account compromise, phishing, and subsequent lateral movement within the network. Furthermore, the lack of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) creates a void in formal guidelines for employee behavior, potentially leading to insecure practices and legal liabilities.

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and strengthen the cybersecurity posture of True Grit, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement MFA for Email Access:

    • Action: Mandate and implement Multi-Factor Authentication for all email accounts across the organization.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of credential theft and unauthorized access to email, a primary vector for cyberattacks.

  2. Develop and Enforce an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Draft, approve, and implement a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines acceptable and unacceptable use of organizational IT resources. Ensure all employees are trained on and acknowledge the policy.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear expectations for employee conduct, reduces human error-related security incidents, and strengthens legal standing in case of misuse.

  3. Urgent Investigation and Remediation of Exposed Sensitive Database on Port 8080:

    • Action: Immediately investigate the service running on . Determine the exact nature of the "TOP SECRET DB," its contents, and the level of sensitivity. Apply immediate access controls such as firewall rules to restrict access to only authorized internal systems or personnel. If the database is not intended for network exposure, it should be secured or shut down.

    • Benefit: Prevents unauthorized access, data exfiltration, and potential compromise of critical organizational data.

  4. Reconcile Risk Register Discrepancies and Enhance Vulnerability Management:

    • Action: Conduct a thorough review of the organization’s risk register, specifically focusing on discrepancies between documented risks and actual technical findings. Implement a robust vulnerability management process that includes regular, authenticated scans, and a formal procedure for updating the risk register based on new technical data. Investigate the root cause of the Port 8080 discrepancy.

    • Benefit: Ensures the risk register accurately reflects the current security posture, improves the effectiveness of risk management, and prevents critical vulnerabilities from being overlooked.

  5. Conduct Regular Security Posture Reviews:

    • Action: Establish a schedule for periodic comprehensive security posture reviews, including vulnerability assessments, penetration testing, and policy audits, to proactively identify and address emerging risks. Benefit: Maintains an up-to-date understanding of the security landscape and ensures continuous improvement of security controls.

Conclusion

True Grit faces several significant cybersecurity challenges, primarily stemming from critical control gaps (MFA for email, AUP) and a severe technical vulnerability involving a potentially exposed sensitive database. The contradiction between technical scan results and the internal risk register for port 8080 is particularly concerning, indicating a need for urgent review of both technical controls and internal risk management processes. Addressing these findings through the recommended actions will substantially enhance True Grit’s overall security posture and resilience against cyber threats.
