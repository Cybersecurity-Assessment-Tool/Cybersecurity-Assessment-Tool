Analysis of Security Control Gaps

The questionnaire responses highlight several critical security control deficiencies:

  • Email MFA Absence: The lack of Multi-Factor Authentication for email access significantly increases the risk of account compromise, phishing attacks, and subsequent unauthorized access to sensitive information.

  • No Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of a formal AUP leaves employees without clear guidelines on appropriate use of company IT resources, increasing risks of misuse, data breaches, and non-compliance.

  • Inadequate Annual Security Training: While new employees receive training, the lack of annual refresher training for all employees means that security awareness may degrade over time, leaving the organization vulnerable to evolving threats.

Risk Assessment

This section correlates the findings from the security control review, technical scan, and pre-existing risk documentation.

Identified Critical Risks and Vulnerabilities

  1. Critical: Exposed "TOP SECRET DB" on Port 8080 (New Finding)

    • Overview: The network scan explicitly identified an open port 8080 hosting a service titled "TOP SECRET DB". This indicates a direct exposure of what appears to be a highly sensitive internal database or system.

    • Severity: Critical. Direct exposure of sensitive data systems is a severe security flaw that can lead to catastrophic data breaches.

    • Correlation with Existing Risks: The organization’s existing risk record (Input 3) lists "Port 8080 Secured" with a severity of 0.0, labelling it as a "false positive." This critical discrepancy indicates that the current security posture regarding port 8080 is severely misjudged internally. This previous assessment is contradicted by the current scan and requires urgent re-evaluation.

  2. High: Lack of MFA for Email Access

    • Overview: Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks. Compromised email accounts often serve as a gateway to broader network access and sensitive data.

    • Severity: High. Email is a primary vector for attacks and often contains sensitive communications and access recovery mechanisms.

  3. High: Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

    • Overview: A lack of a clear AUP creates ambiguity regarding acceptable online behavior, software installation, data handling, and use of company resources, increasing the likelihood of unintentional data leaks, malware infections, and compliance failures.

    • Severity: High. Contributes to an unmanaged risk environment and potential legal liabilities.

  4. Medium: Inadequate Annual Security Awareness Training

    • Overview: While initial training is provided, the absence of regular, annual security awareness training means employees may not be updated on the latest threats (e.g., advanced phishing techniques, social engineering) and best practices. This degrades the human firewall over time.

    • Severity: Medium. Increases susceptibility to social engineering and human-factor vulnerabilities over time.

Recommendations

The following recommendations are provided to address the identified risks and enhance Radiant Life’s cybersecurity posture:

Immediate Actions (Critical)

  • Investigate and Secure Port 8080:

    • Immediately verify the nature of the "TOP SECRET DB" service on 10.5.5.5:8080.

    • Determine if this service should be exposed. If not, block external access to port 8080 on all perimeter firewalls and restrict internal access to only necessary systems.

    • If it is an intended service, ensure it is properly secured with strong authentication, encryption (HTTPS), and authorization controls. Reassess its public exposure.

    • Urgent: Review and update internal risk records for port 8080, correcting the outdated "secured/false positive" assessment.

Short-Term Actions (High)

  • Implement MFA for Email Access:

    • Mandate Multi-Factor Authentication for all employee email accounts without delay. This is a fundamental security control against account compromise.

  • Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Draft, approve, and disseminate a comprehensive AUP for all employees, clearly outlining expectations for the use of company IT resources.

    • Ensure all employees read and acknowledge the AUP.

Long-Term Actions (Medium)

  • Establish Annual Security Awareness Training:

    • Develop and implement a recurring annual security awareness training program for all employees, covering current threats such as phishing, social engineering, data handling, and corporate policies.

    • Track employee completion and understanding of the training.

  • Regular Network Security Assessments:

    • Conduct periodic internal and external network vulnerability scans and penetration tests to proactively identify and remediate security weaknesses.

Conclusion

Radiant Life faces several significant cybersecurity risks, most notably the direct exposure of a "TOP SECRET DB" system and critical gaps in foundational security controls such as MFA for email and an Acceptable Use Policy. The discrepancy between the scan findings and internal risk records for port 8080 indicates a potential blind spot in the organization’s risk management process. Addressing these findings with urgency and commitment will substantially improve the organization’s overall security posture against current and emerging threats.
