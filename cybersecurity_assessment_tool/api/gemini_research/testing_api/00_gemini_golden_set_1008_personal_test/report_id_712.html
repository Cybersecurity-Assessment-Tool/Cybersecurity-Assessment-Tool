Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive cybersecurity posture assessment for Crestview Analytics, drawing insights from a recent network scan, a detailed organizational security questionnaire, and a review of any existing identified risks.

A key positive finding from the external network scan is the absence of any open ports on the target host, significantly reducing the immediate external attack surface. This indicates a well-configured perimeter from a basic port scanning perspective.

However, a critical governance and compliance gap was identified: the organization currently lacks an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). This fundamental policy is essential for establishing clear guidelines for employee conduct regarding company IT assets, data, and internet usage, and its absence poses a notable risk.

No pre-existing vulnerabilities or specific technical misconfigurations were identified from the provided data. Recommendations are provided to address the identified policy gap and further enhance Crestview Analytics’ overall security posture.

Recommendations

To address the identified risk and further strengthen Crestview Analytics’ cybersecurity posture, the following recommendation is provided:

Recommendation 1: Develop and Implement a Comprehensive Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

  • Description: Create and formally implement a written Acceptable Use Policy that clearly outlines the expected and prohibited behaviors for employees regarding the use of all company-owned or controlled IT assets, networks, systems, and data.

  • Justification: An AUP is a fundamental component of an organization’s security governance framework. It sets clear boundaries, communicates employee responsibilities, reduces the risk of unintentional policy violations, and provides a basis for disciplinary action if policies are breached. This policy enhances overall security by mitigating risks associated with human error, insider threats, and compliance failures.

  • Action Steps:

    1. Form a Policy Development Team: Assemble a cross-functional team including representatives from IT, HR, and Legal departments to draft the AUP.

    2. Define Scope and Content: The policy should cover, but not be limited to:

      • Use of company computers, networks, internet, and email.

      • Data handling procedures, including classification, storage, and sharing.

      • Prohibited activities (e.g., illegal content, unauthorized software installation, harassment).

      • Password management guidelines.

      • Incident reporting procedures.

      • Bring Your Own Device (BYOD) policies, if applicable.

      • Consequences of policy violations.

    3. Legal Review and Management Approval: Ensure the AUP is reviewed by legal counsel to ensure compliance with relevant laws and regulations, and obtain formal approval from senior management.

    4. Communication and Acknowledgment: Distribute the approved AUP to all employees. Conduct mandatory training to explain the policy and require all employees to acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to abide by its terms.

    5. Regular Review and Updates: Establish a process for periodically reviewing (e.g., annually) and updating the AUP to ensure it remains relevant with technological changes and evolving regulatory landscapes.

  • Priority: Critical
