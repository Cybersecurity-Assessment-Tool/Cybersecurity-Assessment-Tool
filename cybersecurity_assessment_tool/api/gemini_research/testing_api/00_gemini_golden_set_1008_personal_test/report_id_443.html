Executive Summary

This report details the cybersecurity posture of Skyward Bound based on a review of organizational data, a network scan, and pre-existing risk information. The assessment reveals a mixed security landscape, with some foundational controls in place, but significant gaps in multi-factor authentication (MFA) adoption, employee security awareness, and policy enforcement. A critical internal service (localhost:22) is noted as an existing high-severity risk. Addressing these identified vulnerabilities and implementing recommended controls is crucial to enhancing the overall security posture and reducing exposure to potential threats.

Analysis of Questionnaire Gaps

The questionnaire responses indicate several critical gaps in security controls:

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Logins: Multi-factor authentication is not required for logging into computers, significantly increasing the risk of unauthorized access due to compromised credentials.

  • Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Critical systems holding sensitive data are not protected by MFA, leaving them highly vulnerable to credential theft attacks.

  • Absence of Acceptable Use Policy: The lack of an Acceptable Use Policy means employees may not be formally aware of their responsibilities regarding company IT resources, increasing the risk of misuse or non-compliance.

  • No Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New hires are not receiving initial security awareness training, making them potentially susceptible to common social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing) from day one.

Recommendations

To significantly enhance Skyward Boundâ€™s cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided, categorized by priority:

Immediate Actions

  • Implement MFA for All Critical Systems: Mandate and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all computer logins and access to sensitive data systems without delay. This is a foundational control against credential theft.

  • Address "Localhost Exposed" Risk: Immediately investigate the "Localhost Exposed" risk. Understand why a service on 127.0.0.1 is considered critical and exposed. If it is SSH (port 22), ensure it is properly secured, restricted to necessary users/IPs, and monitored.

Mid-Term Actions

  • Develop and Enforce Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a comprehensive AUP outlining employee responsibilities, acceptable use of company assets, and security expectations. Communicate this policy to all employees and require acknowledgment.

  • Integrate Security Awareness into New Employee Onboarding: Establish mandatory security awareness training as part of the onboarding process for all new employees, covering phishing, password hygiene, and data handling best practices.

  • Perform Detailed Vulnerability Scans: Conduct regular, comprehensive vulnerability scans with service version detection to identify outdated software, known vulnerabilities, and misconfigurations on all internal and external assets.

  • Review Service on Port 22: Investigate the purpose of the service running on port 22 on 127.0.0.1. If not essential or intended to be exposed (even locally), disable it or restrict access.

Long-Term Strategy

  • Continuous Security Training and Awareness: Continue annual security awareness training for all employees, updating content regularly to reflect new threats and best practices.

  • Regular Policy Review: Periodically review and update all security policies (including the AUP) to ensure they remain relevant and effective.

  • Incident Response Planning: Develop and test an incident response plan to ensure the organization can effectively detect, respond to, and recover from security incidents.

  • Regular Security Audits: Engage in periodic third-party security audits to get an objective assessment of the security posture and identify emerging risks.
