Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive cybersecurity posture assessment for Skyward Bound, based on a recent network scan, organizational security questionnaire responses, and pre-existing risk data.

The assessment indicates a mixed security posture. On the positive side, a recent network scan did not identify any open ports on the target 192.168.0.5, and port 80 was explicitly found to be closed. This finding suggests a potential remediation or absence of a previously identified "Unencrypted Web Server" risk on port 80, which requires further verification. Furthermore, Multi-factor Authentication (MFA) is implemented for computer logins and sensitive data systems, and an acceptable use policy is in place with annual security awareness training for all employees.

However, critical gaps were identified through the questionnaire, notably the lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email access, posing a significant risk to account compromise. Additionally, new employees do not receive mandatory security awareness training, which can lead to increased human-factor vulnerabilities early in their tenure. Addressing these policy and training deficiencies, alongside verifying the status of the previously identified technical risk, are key recommendations to uplift Skyward Bound’s security posture.

CR-1 Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access Critical Email accounts are often a primary vector for phishing, business email compromise (BEC), and unauthorized access. Recommendations

The following actionable recommendations are provided to enhance the cybersecurity posture of Skyward Bound, addressing the identified risks and gaps:

Immediate Actions

  • Implement MFA for Email Access (CR-1): Mandate and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication for all email accounts across the organization. This is a critical control for preventing email account compromise, even if passwords are stolen, significantly reducing the attack surface for phishing and BEC.

  • Establish New Employee Security Awareness Training (CR-2): Develop and implement a mandatory, comprehensive security awareness training program for all new hires. This training should be integrated into the onboarding process and cover essential topics such as phishing recognition, password hygiene, acceptable use policy, data handling, and incident reporting from their first day.

Mid-Term Actions

  • Verify Remediation of Port 80 (TR-1): Conduct a thorough investigation to confirm that the "Unencrypted Web Server" risk on Port 80 has been fully remediated and is not present on any other internal or external assets. If any HTTP services are still operational and intended for public access, ensure they are immediately secured with HTTPS using valid SSL/TLS certificates.

  • Regular Vulnerability Scanning: Implement a schedule for regular internal and external vulnerability scanning to proactively identify and address security weaknesses in systems and applications.

Long-Term Strategy

  • Periodic Penetration Testing: Consider conducting periodic penetration testing by independent third parties. This will simulate real-world attacks to identify complex vulnerabilities that automated scanners might miss.

  • Continuous Security Awareness Training Enhancement: Expand the existing annual security awareness training to include more advanced topics, simulated phishing exercises, and role-specific modules to keep all employees updated on evolving threats and best practices.

  • Policy Review and Updates: Periodically review and update the employee acceptable use policy and all other security policies to ensure they remain relevant, effective, and align with current industry best practices and regulatory requirements.
