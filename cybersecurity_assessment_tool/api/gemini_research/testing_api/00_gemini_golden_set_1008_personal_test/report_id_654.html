Analysis of Questionnaire Results

The questionnaire responses indicate several critical deficiencies:

  • MFA Gaps: Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is not required for accessing email or logging into computers. This represents a significant vulnerability, as compromised credentials could lead to unauthorized access to critical systems and data. While MFA is used for sensitive data systems, its absence in general access points severely weakens the overall security perimeter.

  • Policy Deficiencies: The absence of an employee acceptable use policy (AUP) leaves the organization exposed to various insider threats and misuse of company resources. An AUP sets clear expectations for employee conduct regarding technology use, data handling, and security.

  • Training Gaps: Although new employees receive security awareness training, the lack of annual training for all employees means that knowledge may become outdated, and new threats or policies may not be effectively communicated. Ongoing training is crucial for maintaining a strong human firewall.

Risk Assessment

This section consolidates the findings into a comprehensive risk assessment, combining critical gaps from the security control review with pre-existing risks and technical observations.

Identified Risks from Security Control Review

The following high-priority risks were identified based on the questionnaire responses:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access (High Severity): Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks. Compromised email accounts can lead to business email compromise (BEC), data exfiltration, and further network intrusion.

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Login (High Severity): Similar to email, a lack of MFA for computer logins increases the risk of unauthorized access to workstations and internal network resources, especially in scenarios involving lost or stolen devices, or compromised user credentials.

  • Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (Medium Severity): Without a formal AUP, employees may inadvertently or intentionally engage in activities that expose the organization to legal, reputational, or security risks. This lack of clear guidelines can lead to inconsistent behavior and difficulty in enforcing security standards.

  • Lack of Annual Security Awareness Training (Medium Severity): While initial training is beneficial, the absence of regular, annual training means employees may not be up-to-date on the latest threats, phishing techniques, or organizational security policies. This increases the likelihood of human error leading to security incidents.

Recommendations

Based on the analysis, the following recommendations are provided to enhance Willow Creek Health’s cybersecurity posture:

Policy, Training, and Human Factors

  • Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Universally:

    • For Email Access: Mandate MFA for all email accounts. This significantly reduces the risk of email compromise.

    • For Computer Login: Implement MFA for all user logins to company computers and workstations, especially for those accessing sensitive data or administrative privileges.

  • Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines employee responsibilities and permissible uses of company IT resources, data, and communication systems. Ensure all employees read, understand, and acknowledge this policy.

  • Conduct Regular Security Awareness Training: Beyond initial onboarding, implement mandatory annual (or bi-annual) security awareness training for all employees. Training should cover current threats, phishing recognition, data handling best practices, and organizational security policies.

Technical and Risk Management

  • Verify Port 80 Status: Investigate the discrepancy regarding "Unencrypted Web Server" risk. Confirm whether Port 80 is open on any critical system or external interface within the organizational network.

    • If Port 80 is found to be open and serving content, it should be immediately protected by enforcing HTTPS (SSL/TLS encryption) and redirecting all HTTP traffic to HTTPS.

    • If Port 80 is not required, ensure it is properly closed and filtered at the firewall level.

  • Regular Vulnerability Assessments: Conduct periodic vulnerability scans and penetration tests on both internal and external assets to identify and remediate technical weaknesses proactively.

  • Maintain an Accurate Risk Register: Ensure the organization’s risk register is regularly reviewed and updated to reflect the current state of assets, vulnerabilities, and threats. This includes verifying pre-existing risks against current scan data and operational status.

Conclusion

Willow Creek Health demonstrates a foundational understanding of security by implementing MFA for sensitive data systems and initial employee training. However, significant weaknesses exist in the broader application of MFA, policy enforcement, and continuous security education. Addressing these gaps, particularly the implementation of MFA for email and computer access, alongside robust policies and ongoing training, will substantially uplift the organization’s overall cybersecurity posture and resilience against common threats. Proactive verification of recorded risks against current technical scans is also crucial for an accurate security overview.
