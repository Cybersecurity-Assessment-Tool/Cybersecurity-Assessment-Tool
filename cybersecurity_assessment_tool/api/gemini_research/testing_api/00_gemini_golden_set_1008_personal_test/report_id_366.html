Risk Assessment

This section correlates findings from the security control review, technical scans, and pre-existing risk information to provide a comprehensive risk assessment.

Identified Critical Gaps and High Risks (from Questionnaire)

Based on the security control review, the following critical gaps and high risks were identified:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access: Email is a primary vector for phishing and compromise. The absence of MFA significantly increases the risk of account takeover, leading to data breaches, business email compromise (BEC), and unauthorized access to other linked systems.

  • Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): An AUP provides guidelines for employees on the proper use of company resources, data, and systems. Without one, there is an increased risk of misuse, unauthorized actions, and a lack of legal recourse for policy violations.

  • Inadequate Security Awareness Training (New Employees): New employees are often targets for social engineering. Lack of initial training leaves them vulnerable to common attack techniques, potentially compromising the organization from day one.

  • Inadequate Security Awareness Training (Annual for All Employees): Cyber threats evolve constantly. Without regular training, employees may not be aware of new threats, compliance requirements, or best practices, increasing the likelihood of human error leading to security incidents.

Technical Findings and Pre-existing Risks

  • Minimal External Attack Surface: The technical scan of found no open ports. This is a positive finding, indicating that the specific host scanned is not directly exposing services to the network that could be exploited.

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email:

    • Action: Mandate MFA for all email accounts across the organization.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access due to stolen or weak passwords.

  2. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a comprehensive AUP outlining expectations for employees regarding the use of company systems, data, and internet access. Obtain signed acknowledgments from all employees.

    • Benefit: Provides clear guidelines, establishes a baseline for acceptable behavior, and supports legal recourse if policies are violated.

  3. Establish Comprehensive Security Awareness Training Programs:

    • Action (New Employees): Implement mandatory security awareness training as part of the onboarding process for all new hires.

    • Action (Annual Training): Conduct mandatory annual security awareness training for all employees, covering current threats, phishing recognition, data handling, and company policies.

    • Benefit: Educates employees on cybersecurity best practices, reduces the likelihood of successful social engineering attacks, and fosters a security-conscious culture.

  4. Review and Clarify Pre-existing Risks:

    • Action: Investigate the "Unencrypted Web Server" risk (Severity 5.0) to determine if it applies to other assets within the organization. If a web server is indeed operational (or planned) elsewhere, ensure it is configured to use HTTPS/SSL/TLS for all traffic.

    • Benefit: Ensures that risk inventory is accurate and that appropriate security measures are in place for all active web services.

  5. Maintain Regular Security Scanning:

    • Action: Continue periodic network scans and vulnerability assessments to identify any new exposures or misconfigurations as the network evolves.

    • Benefit: Provides ongoing visibility into the external attack surface and helps proactively identify potential vulnerabilities.
