Executive Summary

This report details the cybersecurity posture of Signal Flare based on a review of organizational data, a security questionnaire, and technical network scan results. The assessment reveals several critical security gaps and a significant, high-severity discrepancy in the understanding of existing network services.

Key findings include a lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email and computer access, the absence of an Acceptable Use Policy, and insufficient annual security awareness training. Most critically, network scanning identified an open port 8080 on an internal system () exposing what appears to be a "TOP SECRET DB". This directly contradicts a prior internal assessment indicating this port was secure, highlighting a severe misassessment and active, critical data exposure risk. Immediate remediation efforts are strongly recommended to address these vulnerabilities and significantly enhance overall security.

5. Recommendations

The following actionable recommendations are provided to address the identified vulnerabilities and significantly enhance the overall security posture of Signal Flare.

  • Immediate Remediation for Port 8080 Exposure (Critical):

    • Immediately investigate the service running on . Determine the nature of the "TOP SECRET DB" and whether its exposure is intentional or accidental.

    • If unintentional, shut down the service, block access at the firewall, or restrict network access to only authorized, isolated systems.

    • If intentional, implement robust security controls including strong authentication, granular authorization, and encryption (e.g., HTTPS with valid certificates, strong Access Control Lists, mandatory VPN access).

    • Review and update the organization’s internal risk assessment and vulnerability management procedures to prevent such critical misassessments and ensure accurate security posture representation in the future.

  • Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):

    • Deploy MFA for all email accounts across the organization to significantly reduce the risk of account takeover via phishing or credential stuffing attacks.

    • Implement MFA for all computer logins, especially for administrative accounts, to protect against unauthorized access and lateral movement within the network.

  • Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Create a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines acceptable use of organizational IT resources, data, and systems.

    • Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge the AUP, with regular re-affirmation to maintain compliance awareness.

  • Enhance Security Awareness Training:

    • Implement mandatory annual security awareness training for all employees, not just new hires. This ensures continuous education on evolving threats.

    • Training should cover critical topics such as identifying phishing attempts, social engineering tactics, secure data handling practices, and adherence to organizational security policies.

  • Regular Security Audits and Penetration Testing:

    • Establish a schedule for regular internal and external security audits, including comprehensive network scans and penetration testing, to proactively identify vulnerabilities before they can be exploited.

    • Ensure a formal process for reviewing and addressing findings from these audits.
