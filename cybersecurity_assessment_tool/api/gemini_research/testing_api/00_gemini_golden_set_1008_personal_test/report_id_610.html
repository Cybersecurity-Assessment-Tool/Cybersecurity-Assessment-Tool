Risk Assessment

Based on the analysis of the organizational data and security control review, the following risks have been identified:

Identified Risks
Risk ID Risk Name Overview Severity
R1 Absence of MFA for Email Access Without Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on email accounts, these are highly susceptible to compromise via phishing, brute-force, or credential stuffing attacks. A compromised email can lead to business email compromise, data exfiltration, and further network penetration. Critical
R2 Absence of MFA for Computer Logins Lack of MFA for computer logins significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access to internal systems and data if employee credentials are stolen or guessed. This can lead to lateral movement within the network and severe data breaches. Critical
R3 Lack of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) The absence of an AUP leaves employees without clear guidelines on acceptable use of company IT resources. This increases the risk of misuse, data leakage, legal liabilities, and exposure to malware or unauthorized software installations. High
R4 Infrequent All-Employee Security Awareness Training While new employees receive training, the lack of annual security awareness training for all employees means they may not be updated on current threats, best practices, or evolving company policies. This significantly increases the human element risk, making the organization more vulnerable to social engineering and other attacks. High

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture of Titanium Core, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Universally:

    • Immediate Action: Deploy MFA for all email accounts across the organization without delay. This is a critical first step to protect against credential theft.

    • Expand Coverage: Implement MFA for all computer logins, especially for administrative accounts and access to critical internal systems and applications.

  2. Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Policy Creation: Create a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines employee responsibilities and acceptable conduct regarding company IT resources, internet usage, data handling, and software installation.

    • Employee Acknowledgment: Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge the AUP. Integrate it into onboarding processes and annual reviews.

  3. Enhance Security Awareness Training Program:

    • Annual Training: Implement mandatory annual (or more frequent) security awareness training for all employees, not just new hires.

    • Content Update: Training content should be regularly updated to cover current threats such as sophisticated phishing techniques, ransomware, social engineering tactics, and data privacy best practices.

  4. Conduct Regular Vulnerability Scanning and Penetration Testing:

    • Proactive Identification: Perform regular external and internal vulnerability scans on all public-facing and internal assets to proactively identify and remediate technical weaknesses.

    • Deeper Insight: Include authenticated scans for deeper insight into system configurations, patch levels, and software vulnerabilities. Consider engaging in periodic penetration testing to simulate real-world attacks.
