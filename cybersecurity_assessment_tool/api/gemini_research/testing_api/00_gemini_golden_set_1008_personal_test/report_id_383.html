Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive cybersecurity posture assessment for Firebrand Media, based on network scan data, organizational security questionnaire responses, and existing risk registers. The assessment reveals several critical security gaps and high-severity risks that require immediate attention.

Key findings include a significant lack of multi-factor authentication (MFA) implementation for critical systems like email and sensitive data access, the absence of fundamental security policies such as an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP), and a complete lack of security awareness training for employees. Furthermore, an exposed internal service on (localhost), identified as "Localhost Exposed" with a critical severity score, presents a potential internal attack vector. Addressing these deficiencies is paramount to strengthening Firebrand Media’s overall security resilience.

Analysis of Questionnaire Gaps

The "No" responses highlighted in Table 1 represent significant vulnerabilities and control gaps that could be exploited by malicious actors or lead to internal security incidents:

  • MFA for Email Access: The lack of MFA for email access is a critical vulnerability. Email accounts are frequent targets for phishing and account takeover attempts. Without MFA, a compromised password grants full access, potentially leading to business email compromise, data exfiltration, or further internal network penetration.

  • MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Similar to email, not requiring MFA for sensitive data systems significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access to critical organizational data, intellectual property, or customer information.

  • Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of an AUP leaves employees without clear guidelines on acceptable use of company IT resources, data handling, and security expectations. This can lead to increased human error, data leakage, and legal liabilities.

  • Security Awareness Training: The complete lack of security awareness training (for both new and existing employees) is a major risk factor. Employees are often the first line of defense; without proper training, they are more susceptible to social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing, vishing) and may inadvertently introduce malware or mishandle sensitive information.

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and improve Firebrand Media’s overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email:

    • Mandate MFA for all user accounts accessing email services.

    • Evaluate and implement a robust MFA solution (e.g., TOTP, FIDO2, push notifications).

    • Provide clear instructions and support for employee enrollment and usage.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Enforce MFA requirements for all access to systems containing sensitive data.

    • Prioritize critical systems and systematically roll out MFA across all relevant platforms.

  3. Develop and Enforce an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Create a comprehensive AUP outlining acceptable use of company IT resources, internet, email, and data handling practices.

    • Ensure all employees review and acknowledge the AUP, preferably annually.

    • Integrate AUP review into the new employee onboarding process.

  4. Implement Mandatory Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Develop and deliver essential security awareness training as part of the new employee onboarding process.

    • Cover topics such as phishing, password hygiene, data handling, and reporting suspicious activities.

  5. Implement Mandatory Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees:

    • Establish an annual security awareness training program for all employees to reinforce best practices and educate on emerging threats.

    • Utilize engaging and relevant content, including simulated phishing exercises.

  6. Review and Secure Exposed Localhost Service:

    • Investigate the service running on port 22 on .

    • Determine if the service is necessary and if its exposure is intended.

    • If necessary, ensure it is secured with strong, unique credentials, updated to the latest secure version, and configured with least privilege principles.

    • If not necessary, disable or remove the service.
