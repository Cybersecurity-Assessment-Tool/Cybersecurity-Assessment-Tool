Executive Summary

This report details the cybersecurity posture assessment for Obsidian Operatives. The assessment was conducted using a combination of organizational questionnaire responses and an attempt at technical network scanning, along with a review of pre-existing risk data.

Due to critical issues with the provided technical scan data and pre-existing risk data (both indicated as ‘[BROKEN]‘), a comprehensive technical analysis was not possible, and no pre-existing vulnerabilities could be evaluated. The assessment primarily relies on the organization’s self-reported security control implementation.

Key findings indicate significant security gaps related to Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email and sensitive data systems, a lack of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP), and insufficient security awareness training for new employees. These deficiencies expose the organization to elevated risks of account compromise, unauthorized data access, and human-factor vulnerabilities. Immediate attention to these areas is strongly recommended to enhance the overall security posture.

Risk Assessment

Identified Risks

Based on the security questionnaire responses, the following risks have been identified. Note that this assessment is limited due to the unavailability of technical scan data and pre-existing risk information.

Risk Name Overview Severity
Lack of MFA for Email Access The organization does not require Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for email access, significantly increasing the risk of account compromise through phishing, credential stuffing, or brute-force attacks. High
Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems Absence of MFA for systems handling sensitive data exposes critical information to unauthorized access if a single factor (e.g., password) is compromised. High
Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) Without an AUP, employees may engage in activities that expose the organization to legal, operational, and security risks, lacking clear guidelines on appropriate technology usage. Medium
Insufficient New Employee Security Awareness Training New employees who do not receive immediate security awareness training are more susceptible to social engineering, phishing, and malware, posing an immediate risk to the organization’s security from day one. Medium

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture of Obsidian Operatives, the following recommendations are provided. These are prioritized based on the severity of the associated risks.

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Action: Mandate MFA for all email accounts, especially for administrators and privileged users.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise, even if passwords are stolen.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Action: Deploy and enforce MFA for all systems and applications that store, process, or transmit sensitive organizational data.

    • Benefit: Provides an additional layer of security, protecting critical data from unauthorized access.

  3. Develop and Enforce an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a clear, comprehensive AUP outlining acceptable use of company IT resources, internet, email, and data handling. Distribute it to all employees and require acknowledgment.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear expectations for employee conduct, reduces misuse of resources, and mitigates legal and security risks.

  4. Implement Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Action: Integrate mandatory security awareness training as part of the onboarding process for all new hires before they gain access to company systems.

    • Benefit: Equips new employees with essential cybersecurity knowledge from the start, making them more resilient to common threats like phishing and social engineering.

  5. Conduct a Comprehensive External Network Scan:

    • Action: Perform a thorough external vulnerability scan and penetration test against the organization’s public-facing infrastructure (58.69.26.119 and ).

    • Benefit: Identifies exposed services, outdated software, and misconfigurations that could be exploited by attackers, providing a full technical risk overview.
