Analysis of Security Controls

Copperhead Cables demonstrates a commendable commitment to several foundational security practices, including the widespread adoption of MFA for computer logins and sensitive data systems, a well-defined acceptable use policy, and a consistent schedule for security awareness training for all employees. These measures collectively contribute to a stronger human and policy-based security posture.

However, a critical vulnerability exists due to the lack of MFA enforcement for email access. Email remains a primary target for sophisticated phishing attacks, business email compromise (BEC) schemes, and malware distribution. Without MFA, a simple compromise of credentials (e.g., via phishing) can grant attackers full access to an employee’s mailbox, potentially leading to financial fraud, sensitive data exposure, or further penetration into internal systems. This represents the most significant identified security gap.

Recommendations

To fortify Copperhead Cables’ cybersecurity posture and mitigate the identified risks, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access (Critical Priority):

    • Action: Immediately enable and enforce MFA for all user accounts accessing corporate email services (e.g., Microsoft 365, Google Workspace).

    • Justification: This is the most critical identified gap. MFA significantly reduces the risk of account compromise due to stolen or weak passwords, especially for email, which is a common initial attack vector.

    • Consideration: Educate employees on the importance of MFA and provide clear instructions for enrollment and usage. Explore phishing-resistant MFA methods (e.g., FIDO2 security keys) for high-privilege accounts.

  2. Conduct Comprehensive External Network Scans:

    • Action: Schedule and execute regular, in-depth vulnerability scans on all external-facing IP addresses and domains.

    • Justification: The current assessment lacked technical scan data. Comprehensive scans are vital to identify open ports, insecure services, outdated software versions, and other technical vulnerabilities that could be exploited by attackers.

    • Consideration: Include authenticated and unauthenticated scans, web application vulnerability scanning, and consider engaging a third-party for periodic penetration testing to simulate real-world attacks.

  3. Develop a Formal Vulnerability Management Program:

    • Action: Establish a structured process for identifying, assessing, prioritizing, and remediating vulnerabilities across all IT assets.

    • Justification: A formal program ensures that vulnerabilities, once identified, are addressed systematically and within acceptable timeframes, reducing the overall attack surface.

    • Consideration: Implement tools for continuous monitoring of systems, define clear Service Level Agreements (SLAs) for patching, and integrate vulnerability data into a risk management framework.

  4. Enhance Incident Response Planning for Email Compromise:

    • Action: Review and update the existing incident response plan to specifically address scenarios involving email account compromise and Business Email Compromise (BEC).

    • Justification: Given the identified risk, the organization must be prepared to detect, contain, eradicate, and recover from email-related security incidents swiftly and effectively.

    • Consideration: Conduct regular tabletop exercises to test the plan’s effectiveness, train IT staff on specific email compromise response procedures, and establish clear communication protocols for affected users and external parties.
