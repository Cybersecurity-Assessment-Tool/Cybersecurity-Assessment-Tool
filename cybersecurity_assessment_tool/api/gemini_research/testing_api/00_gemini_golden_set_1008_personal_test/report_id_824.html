Analysis of Security Control Gaps

The review highlights critical deficiencies in foundational security practices:

  • Absence of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA is not required for email access, computer logins, or sensitive data systems. This is a severe vulnerability, as compromised credentials (e.g., through phishing or brute force) could grant attackers full access to critical organizational resources with minimal effort. MFA is a cornerstone of modern identity and access management and significantly reduces the risk of account compromise.

  • Lack of Security Awareness Training: The organization does not conduct security awareness training for new employees or annual refresher training for all employees. This leaves the workforce highly susceptible to social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing, pretexting, malware delivery) and reduces their ability to identify and report potential security incidents. Human error is a leading cause of security breaches, and ongoing training is essential to mitigate this risk.

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): While an AUP is in place, its effectiveness is diminished without accompanying security awareness training to ensure employees understand and adhere to its principles.

The presence of an open Port 80, typically used for unencrypted web traffic, on an internal network segment requires further investigation. Without service or version information, it is impossible to definitively determine if this represents a vulnerable application or service. However, any open port on an internal network presents a potential attack surface. Identified Critical Risks

The most pressing risks for Iron Bridge Legal are:

  • Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): This is the single most critical vulnerability identified. Without MFA, a simple phishing attack or brute-force attempt against weak passwords can lead to complete compromise of user accounts and subsequent unauthorized access to internal systems and data. This poses an immediate and severe threat to data confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

  • Insufficient Security Awareness Training: A well-trained workforce is the first line of defense against many cyber threats. The absence of both initial and annual security training leaves employees as significant vectors for attacks, such as ransomware delivery via malicious email attachments or credential harvesting through deceptive websites.

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and improve the overall cybersecurity posture of Iron Bridge Legal, the following actionable recommendations are provided.

Immediate Actions (High Priority)

  • Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):

    • Mandate MFA for all email accounts, especially for administrators and users with access to sensitive information.

    • Implement MFA for all computer logins, particularly for privileged accounts and devices accessing sensitive data.

    • Deploy MFA for access to all sensitive data systems and applications where feasible.

  • Initiate Basic Security Awareness Training:

    • Conduct immediate mandatory security awareness training for all employees, focusing on phishing recognition, strong password best practices, safe internet usage, and the importance of reporting suspicious activity.

    • Ensure all new hires receive security awareness training as part of their onboarding process.

  • Investigate Open Port 80:

    • Immediately identify the service running on Port 80 on 172.16.0.1.

    • Determine if the service is necessary for business operations. If not, close the port or remove the service.

    • If the service is necessary, ensure the service and underlying operating system are fully patched and configured securely. If sensitive information is exchanged, consider migrating to HTTPS (Port 443) with a valid SSL/TLS certificate.

Long-Term Strategies (Medium Priority)

  • Develop a Comprehensive Security Awareness Program:

    • Implement a recurring annual security awareness training program for all employees, covering evolving threats, internal policies, and best practices.

    • Conduct regular phishing simulations to test employee vigilance and reinforce training effectiveness.

  • Regular Vulnerability Scanning and Penetration Testing:

    • Implement a schedule for regular internal and external vulnerability scans to proactively identify and address weaknesses across the network and systems.

    • Consider periodic penetration testing to simulate real-world attacks and assess the effectiveness of implemented security controls.

  • Establish a Robust Patch Management Policy:

    • Develop and enforce a comprehensive patch management policy for all operating systems, applications, and network devices to ensure timely application of security updates and patches.

  • Develop and Test a Data Backup and Recovery Plan:

    • Create and regularly test a comprehensive data backup and disaster recovery plan to ensure business continuity and data resilience in the event of a breach, ransomware attack, or data loss.

  • Review and Update Security Policies:

    • Periodically review and update the Acceptable Use Policy and other security policies (e.g., password policy, data handling policy) to reflect current threats, regulatory requirements, and organizational needs.
