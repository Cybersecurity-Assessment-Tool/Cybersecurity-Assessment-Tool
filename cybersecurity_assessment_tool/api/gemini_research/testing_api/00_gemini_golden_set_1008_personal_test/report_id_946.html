Risk Assessment

Based on the analysis of the provided data, the following risks have been identified. These are primarily derived from the self-assessment questionnaire.

Identified Risks and Severities
Risk Name Overview Severity
Lack of MFA for Email Access The organization does not require Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for accessing email accounts. This significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access due to compromised credentials, leading to phishing, business email compromise, and data breaches. High
Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is not enforced for accessing systems containing sensitive data. This exposes critical information to unauthorized access if primary credentials are stolen or guessed, increasing the risk of data loss or compliance violations. High
Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy The organization lacks an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). Without clear guidelines on appropriate use of company IT assets and data, employees may inadvertently or intentionally engage in risky behaviors, leading to security incidents, data leakage, and legal liabilities. Medium

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement MFA for Email Access:

    • Action: Immediately implement and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all employee email accounts.

    • Justification: This is a critical control to prevent unauthorized access even if credentials are compromised, drastically reducing the risk of phishing and business email compromise.

    • Priority: High

  2. Implement MFA for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Action: Deploy and require MFA for access to all systems, applications, and databases that store or process sensitive organizational data.

    • Justification: Protecting sensitive information with an additional authentication factor is crucial for data integrity, confidentiality, and compliance with regulations.

    • Priority: High

  3. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a comprehensive Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) that clearly defines the rules and expectations for employee conduct when using company IT resources, internet access, email, and handling data. Distribute it to all employees and obtain signed acknowledgments.

    • Justification: An AUP establishes clear boundaries, minimizes insider threats (accidental or malicious), reduces legal risks, and reinforces a culture of security awareness.

    • Priority: Medium
