4. Risk Assessment

This section correlates findings from the questionnaire, technical scans, and pre-existing risk data to provide a comprehensive view of the organization’s risk landscape.

4.1. Identified Critical Gaps from Questionnaire

Based on the security control review, the following critical gaps significantly increase the organization’s risk exposure:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access: Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to phishing and credential stuffing attacks, leading to potential data breaches and business email compromise. This represents a significant attack vector.

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Login: Compromised user credentials could grant attackers unauthorized access to internal systems and networks, potentially leading to lateral movement and privilege escalation.

  • Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Critical business data residing in sensitive systems is at heightened risk of unauthorized access or exfiltration without strong authentication layers.

  • Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy: Without clear guidelines and boundaries, employees may engage in activities that inadvertently introduce security risks, such as using unapproved software, accessing malicious websites, or sharing sensitive information insecurely.

  • Lack of Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New hires are often prime targets for social engineering attacks due to lack of familiarity with organizational security protocols, creating a significant vulnerability during the onboarding phase.

4.2. Technical Vulnerabilities

  • Open Port 22 (SSH) on : Port 22, commonly used for SSH, was found open on the target. While ‘127.0.0.1‘ typically refers to a loopback interface, meaning the service is only accessible from the host itself, if this system is part of a larger network and exposed through other means (e.g., specific network configurations, VPN access, or if the service is bound to ‘0.0.0.0‘ and accessible from other network interfaces), it represents a potential entry point. 5. Recommendations

Based on the analysis, the following recommendations are provided to improve Nova Terra’s cybersecurity posture:

5.1. Immediate Actions (High Priority)

  • Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):

    • Immediately enable and enforce MFA for all email accounts across the organization.

    • Implement MFA for all computer logins, with particular emphasis on administrative accounts and endpoints accessing sensitive data.

    • Deploy MFA for access to all sensitive data systems and applications.

  • Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a clear and comprehensive AUP for employees regarding technology usage, data handling, and internet browsing. Ensure all employees formally acknowledge and adhere to this policy.

  • Mandatory Security Awareness Training for New Employees: Integrate comprehensive security awareness training into the onboarding process for all new hires to instill security best practices from day one.

  • Investigate and Secure Open Port 22 on :

    • Determine the exact service running on Port 22 and its purpose.

    • If SSH access is not required, ensure the service is disabled or blocked.

    • If SSH access is necessary, implement stringent security measures: use strong, unique passwords or, preferably, public key authentication; disable root login; restrict access to specific IP addresses; and ensure all SSH configurations adhere to best practices.

  • Address “Localhost Exposed” Critical Risk (CVSS 10.0): Investigate the nature and scope of this critical risk immediately. Determine how the service on ‘127.0.0.1‘ is becoming exposed or exploitable externally. This is paramount for preventing a potential total system compromise. Prioritize containment and remediation.

5.2. Long-term Strategic Recommendations

  • Regular Security Audits and Assessments: Conduct periodic internal and external vulnerability assessments and penetration tests to continuously identify and address new risks and validate the effectiveness of implemented controls.

  • Continuous Security Awareness Program: Maintain annual security awareness training for all employees and integrate ongoing security reminders, simulated phishing campaigns, and topical alerts to foster a strong security culture.

  • Robust Patch Management Program: Implement a formal and proactive patch management program to ensure all operating systems, applications, and network devices are kept up-to-date with the latest security patches.

  • Comprehensive Incident Response Plan (IRP): Develop, document, and regularly test an incident response plan to ensure the organization can effectively detect, respond to, and recover from security incidents in a timely manner.

  • Secure Data Backup and Recovery Strategy: Ensure regular, secure, and tested backups of all critical data are performed, stored off-site or in immutable storage, and validated for restorability.

Conclusion

Nova Terra faces significant cybersecurity challenges stemming from a lack of fundamental security controls, particularly in MFA implementation, security policy enforcement, and new employee training. The identified open port and pre-existing critical "Localhost Exposed" risk further underscore the urgency for remediation. By diligently implementing the recommended actions, Nova Terra can substantially improve its security posture, reduce its attack surface, and enhance its resilience against evolving cyber threats.
