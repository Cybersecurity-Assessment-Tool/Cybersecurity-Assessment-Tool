Analysis of Gaps

  • No MFA for Email: This is a critical vulnerability. Email accounts are a primary target for phishing and account takeover attacks. A compromised email account can lead to data breaches, financial fraud, and further system compromise.

  • No Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of an AUP creates ambiguity regarding the proper use of company assets. This can lead to unintentional insider threats, legal liabilities, and inconsistent security practices.

  • No Annual Security Training: Security threats evolve constantly. Without regular, recurring training, employee awareness of new tactics like sophisticated phishing and social engineering diminishes, making them more susceptible to attacks.

Findings

The scan revealed that port 22 (SSH) is open to the internet. SSH (Secure Shell) is a common protocol for remote system administration. While necessary for management, its public exposure constitutes a significant attack surface.

  • Risk: Exposed administrative services are prime targets for brute-force attacks, credential stuffing, and exploitation of potential vulnerabilities in the SSH server software.

  • Correlation: This risk is amplified by the lack of MFA on email. If an attacker compromises an email account, they may find credentials or information that could be used to attack this exposed SSH service.

Note: Service, product, and version details were not available in the scan data. A more in-depth, authenticated scan is recommended to identify the specific software version and its patch level.

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks, the following actions are recommended in order of priority:

  1. Remediate "Localhost Exposed" Vulnerability (Critical): Immediately investigate and remediate the pre-existing risk documented as "Localhost Exposed." A vulnerability with a CVSS score of 10.0 represents the highest possible level of risk and must be treated as an emergency.

  2. Enforce MFA on All Email Accounts (Critical): Immediately enable and enforce MFA for all users accessing the email system. This is the single most effective control to prevent email account takeovers.

  3. Secure the Exposed SSH Service (High):

    • Review Necessity: Determine if SSH access from the public internet is required. If not, block it at the firewall.

    • Implement Access Controls: If required, restrict access to a whitelist of trusted IP addresses.

    • Enforce Strong Authentication: Disable password-based authentication and mandate the use of SSH keys.

  4. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (High): Create a formal AUP that clearly defines the rules and responsibilities for all employees when using company technology and data. Require all employees to read and acknowledge the policy.

  5. Establish an Annual Security Awareness Program (High): Implement a mandatory security awareness training program for all employees to be completed at least once per year. The training should cover current threats such as phishing, malware, and social engineering.

Conclusion

The current security posture of [Organization Name] requires immediate attention. The combination of critical policy gaps, an exposed administrative service, and a pre-existing critical vulnerability creates a high-risk environment. By implementing the prioritized recommendations in this report, the organization can significantly reduce its attack surface, improve its resilience against common cyber threats, and build a stronger security foundation.
