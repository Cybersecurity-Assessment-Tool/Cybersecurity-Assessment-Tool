3.1 Questionnaire Analysis

The questionnaire responses highlight critical gaps in the organization’s security posture:

  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA is not required for email access, computer logins, or sensitive data systems. This is a severe vulnerability, as compromised credentials (e.g., via phishing) could grant attackers full access to critical organizational resources.

  • Security Awareness Training: The organization does not conduct security awareness training for new employees or on an annual basis for all employees. This leaves employees susceptible to social engineering attacks, phishing, and other human-centric threats, as they may not be equipped to recognize and report malicious activity.

  • Acceptable Use Policy: The presence of an acceptable use policy is positive, providing a baseline for employee conduct regarding IT resources. However, without training, its effectiveness may be limited.

6. Recommendations

To address the identified gaps and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture of , the following recommendations are provided:

6.1 Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

  1. Implement MFA for All Email Accounts: Mandate MFA for all user and administrative email accounts immediately. Prioritize accounts with access to sensitive information or administrative privileges.

  2. Implement MFA for Computer Logins: Deploy MFA solutions for all user and administrative computer logins, especially for devices accessing critical business data or connected to the corporate network.

  3. Implement MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Enforce strong MFA for access to all systems containing sensitive organizational or customer data. This should be a top priority.

  4. Review MFA Solutions: Evaluate and select an MFA solution that integrates well with existing infrastructure and provides a good user experience to ensure adoption.

6.2 Security Awareness Training

  1. Mandatory New Employee Training: Develop and implement a comprehensive security awareness training program for all new employees before they gain full access to IT systems.

  2. Annual All-Employee Training: Establish a mandatory annual security awareness training program for all employees. This training should cover current threats, phishing recognition, data handling, and company policies.

  3. Regular Phishing Simulations: Conduct regular, unannounced phishing simulations to test employee awareness and provide immediate, targeted training to those who fall victim.

6.3 Technical Visibility & Risk Management (General)

  1. Conduct Regular External Network Scans: Implement a routine schedule for external vulnerability scanning and penetration testing to identify and remediate exposed services and misconfigurations. Ensure the scan results are properly stored and accessible for analysis.

  2. Vulnerability Management Program: Establish a formal vulnerability management program to identify, assess, prioritize, and remediate technical vulnerabilities across all systems.

  3. Asset Inventory: Maintain an up-to-date inventory of all IT assets, including their purpose, location, owner, and critical security requirements.

7. Conclusion

While a full technical assessment was hampered by data limitations, the review of organizational controls clearly indicates significant and critical security gaps, particularly concerning Multi-Factor Authentication and Security Awareness Training. Addressing these foundational weaknesses should be the immediate priority for to significantly improve its resilience against common cyber threats. Proactive implementation of the recommended security controls will be crucial for protecting organizational assets and data.
