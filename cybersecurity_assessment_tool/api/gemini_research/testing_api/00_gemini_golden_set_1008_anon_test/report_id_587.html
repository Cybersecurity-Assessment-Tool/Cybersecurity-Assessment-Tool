New Risks Identified from Questionnaire

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access:

    • Overview: Failure to require Multi-Factor Authentication for email access significantly increases the risk of account compromise through various attack vectors such as phishing, brute-force attacks, or credential stuffing. Compromised email accounts are a primary vector for business email compromise (BEC), data breaches, and further internal system compromise.

    • Severity: High

  • Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Overview: Similar to email systems, sensitive data systems that do not enforce MFA are highly vulnerable to unauthorized access. A breach of these systems can result in severe data loss, regulatory non-compliance, significant financial penalties, and extensive reputational damage.

    • Severity: High

  • Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Overview: Without a formal AUP, employees may be unaware of expected behavior regarding the use of organizational IT resources, data handling procedures, and essential security best practices. This lack of guidance can inadvertently lead to security incidents, malware infections, and compliance issues.

    • Severity: Medium

  • Insufficient Annual Security Awareness Training:

    • Overview: While new employees receive training, the absence of regular, annual security awareness training for all employees means that critical security awareness knowledge may degrade over time, and staff may not be updated on emerging threats, evolving policies, and new attack techniques. Human error remains a leading cause of security breaches.

    • Severity: Medium

New Risks Identified from Technical Scan

  • Open SSH Service (Port 22) without Version Details:

    • Overview: An open SSH service provides a potential entry point for attackers if not properly secured. Without specific version information from the scan, it is impossible to determine if the service is running a known vulnerable or outdated version. Weak credentials, default configurations, or unpatched vulnerabilities within the SSH daemon could lead to remote code execution or unauthorized access to the target system.

    • Severity: Medium (Potential - requires further investigation)

Recommendations

The following recommendations are provided to mitigate the identified risks and improve the overall cybersecurity posture of [Organization Name]. Prioritization should be given to Critical and High severity items.

Immediate Actions (Critical & High Risks)

  • Address “Localhost Exposed” Vulnerability: Immediately investigate the nature and scope of the "Localhost Exposed" vulnerability (CVSS 10.0) and apply the necessary fixes. This is the highest priority and requires urgent attention to prevent severe compromise.

  • Implement MFA for Email Access: Mandate Multi-Factor Authentication for all email accounts across the organization. This is a fundamental control that significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise.

  • Implement MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Extend MFA requirements to all systems and applications handling sensitive data. Identify these critical systems and configure MFA as a mandatory login requirement to protect sensitive information.

Medium to Long-Term Actions

  • Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a comprehensive AUP outlining the proper use of company IT resources, acceptable data handling practices, and internet usage guidelines. Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge this policy.

  • Implement Annual Security Awareness Training: Establish a program for mandatory annual security awareness training for all employees. This training should cover current cyber threats (e.g., phishing, ransomware), best practices for identifying malicious content, data protection principles, and policy refreshers.

  • Secure SSH Service (Port 22):

    • Conduct a more detailed scan or an authenticated audit to determine the specific SSH service version and its current configuration.

    • Ensure the SSH server is running the latest patched version to address any known vulnerabilities.

    • Implement strong SSH configurations: disable root login, disable password authentication in favor of key-based authentication, enforce strong passphrases for SSH keys, limit access to trusted IP addresses, and consider changing the default SSH port from 22.

    • Implement rate-limiting and intrusion detection systems (IDS) or intrusion prevention systems (IPS) to protect against brute-force attacks targeting the SSH service.

  • Conduct Regular Vulnerability Scanning and Penetration Testing: Schedule regular external and internal vulnerability scans, along with periodic penetration tests, to proactively identify and address security weaknesses before they can be exploited.

  • Establish Incident Response Plan: Develop, document, and regularly test a formal incident response plan. This plan should outline clear procedures for how the organization will effectively detect, respond to, contain, eradicate, and recover from security incidents.
