Detailed Risk Findings

  • Risk Name: Lack of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

    • Overview: The organization currently lacks an established acceptable use policy for its employees. This policy is a cornerstone of information security, defining the boundaries for employee behavior concerning company information systems, software, hardware, networks, and data. Without a clear AUP, employees may not understand their responsibilities, leading to unintentional or intentional misuse of company resources. This gap creates an elevated risk of incidents such as data exfiltration, introduction of malware, unauthorized software installations, and non-compliance with industry regulations or privacy laws.

    • Severity: High

    • Impact: Financial loss (e.g., fines, recovery costs), reputational damage, legal/regulatory penalties, data breaches, operational disruption, and increased exposure to insider threats.

    • Likelihood: Moderate. While not all employees will act maliciously, the absence of clear guidelines increases the likelihood of human error or opportunistic misuse leading to security incidents.

Recommendations

Based on the analysis, the following recommendations are provided to significantly enhance the cybersecurity posture of [Organization Name].

  1. Develop and Implement a Comprehensive Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Immediately prioritize the creation and implementation of a formal AUP.

    • Details: The AUP should clearly define acceptable and unacceptable use of all company IT assets (hardware, software, networks, data, email, internet access, mobile devices). It should cover topics such as data classification, privacy expectations, software installation guidelines, social media usage on company time/devices, and consequences for non-compliance.

    • Integration: Ensure all new employees are required to read and sign the AUP during onboarding. Conduct annual refresher training for all staff, incorporating the AUP.

  2. Conduct Regular and Comprehensive Network Vulnerability Scans:

    • Action: Establish a regular schedule (e.g., quarterly for internal, monthly for external perimeter) for authenticated vulnerability scans of all network assets.

    • Details: Engage a reputable security vendor or utilize internal tools to perform deep scans that identify not only network-level vulnerabilities but also configuration weaknesses in operating systems and applications.

    • Follow-up: All identified vulnerabilities, especially those rated Critical or High, must be prioritized for remediation.

  3. Establish a Robust Vulnerability Management Program:

    • Action: Implement a systematic process for the entire vulnerability lifecycle: identification, assessment, prioritization, remediation, and verification.

    • Details: Maintain an accurate, up-to-date inventory of all IT assets. Utilize a centralized system to track vulnerabilities from discovery to closure. Define clear roles and responsibilities for vulnerability management activities.

  4. Maintain and Enhance Existing Security Strengths:

    • Action: Continue the excellent practice of requiring MFA for critical systems.

    • Details: Regularly review and update MFA policies and technologies to stay ahead of evolving threats. Ensure security awareness training remains current, engaging, and relevant to the organization’s threat landscape, incorporating new policies like the AUP.
