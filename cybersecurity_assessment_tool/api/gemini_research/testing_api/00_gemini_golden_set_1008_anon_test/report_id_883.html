Risk Assessment

Based on the analysis of the organizational data, security questionnaire, and technical scan results, the following risks have been identified. No pre-existing vulnerabilities were provided.

Identified Risks

  1. Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems

    • Overview: The organization does not enforce MFA for access to systems containing sensitive data. This significantly increases the risk of unauthorized data access if credentials are compromised through phishing, malware, or other means.

    • Severity: High

  2. Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

    • Overview: Without a formal AUP, employees may be unaware of their responsibilities regarding the proper use of organizational IT assets, data, and networks. This can lead to increased risks of data leakage, malware infections, and compliance violations.

    • Severity: High

  3. Insufficient Annual Security Awareness Training

    • Overview: While new employees receive initial security training, the lack of annual refreshers means that employees may not be updated on evolving threats, new company policies, or best practices. This can lead to a degradation of security awareness over time.

    • Severity: Medium

  4. Open Service Port (Port 22 - SSH) on Target

    • Overview: An open SSH port on the target IP represents a potential entry point for attackers. Without information on SSH version, configuration (e.g., password vs. key-based authentication, rate limiting), and access restrictions, it poses a risk of unauthorized access or service disruption.

    • Severity: Medium

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and enhance [Organization Name]’s cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement MFA for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Action: Mandate Multi-Factor Authentication for all access to systems containing sensitive data.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access even if primary credentials are stolen.

  2. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a comprehensive AUP outlining the appropriate use of IT resources, internet, email, and data handling. Ensure all employees review and acknowledge the policy annually.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear expectations for employee conduct, reduces misuse of resources, and strengthens the legal and compliance posture.

  3. Establish Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees:

    • Action: Implement a mandatory annual security awareness training program for all employees, covering current threats (e.g., phishing, ransomware), organizational policies, and best practices.

    • Benefit: Keeps employees informed about evolving threats, reinforces secure behaviors, and strengthens the human firewall.

  4. Review and Secure Open SSH Service (Port 22):

    • Action: Conduct a thorough review of the SSH service configuration on . Ensure it uses strong, key-based authentication, disable password authentication, limit access to specific source IPs, and ensure the SSH server software is up-to-date. If not strictly necessary, consider disabling the service or restricting it to internal networks only.

    • Benefit: Reduces the attack surface and prevents unauthorized remote access attempts.

Conclusion

While [Organization Name] demonstrates some foundational security practices (MFA for email and computers, new employee training), critical vulnerabilities remain. The lack of MFA for sensitive data, absence of an AUP, and insufficient recurring security training pose significant risks. Additionally, the open SSH port requires immediate review. Addressing these issues through the recommended actions will substantially improve the organization’s defensive capabilities against common cyber threats and enhance its overall security posture.
