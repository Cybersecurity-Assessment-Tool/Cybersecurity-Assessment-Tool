5. Risk Assessment

Based on the available information, the following critical gaps and risks have been identified. The absence of comprehensive technical scan data and existing vulnerability lists prevents a complete and holistic risk assessment.

Identified Critical Gaps and High Risks (from Questionnaire)

  1. Lack of MFA for Computer Login:

    • Severity: Critical

    • Overview: The absence of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for computer logins dramatically increases the risk of unauthorized access to internal systems. If an attacker compromises a user’s password through phishing, brute-force, or credential stuffing, they gain direct access to the internal network without an additional authentication factor. This facilitates lateral movement, data exfiltration, and ransomware deployment.

  2. No Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Severity: High

    • Overview: New employees represent a significant vulnerability if not adequately trained on cybersecurity best practices upon onboarding. They are more susceptible to social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing, vishing), malware downloads, and policy violations due to unfamiliarity with the organization’s security protocols and common threats. This increases the likelihood of human error leading to security incidents.

6. Recommendations

The following recommendations are provided to address the identified gaps and to enable a more comprehensive future assessment.

Immediate Actions

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for All Computer Logins:

    • Priority: Critical

    • Details: Mandate MFA for all user accounts accessing company computers and internal networks. This should include both local logins (if applicable) and remote access. Options include hardware tokens, mobile authenticator apps, or biometrics.

  2. Establish Mandatory Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Priority: High

    • Details: Develop and implement a robust security awareness training program that all new employees must complete during their onboarding process. This training should cover phishing, malware, password best practices, acceptable use policies, and incident reporting procedures.

Data Remediation and Further Assessment

  1. Provide Complete and Valid Network Scan Data:

    • Priority: High

    • Details: Conduct a comprehensive network scan and provide the results in a valid, parseable format. This is crucial for identifying exposed services, outdated software, and known vulnerabilities on internet-facing assets and internal systems.

  2. Submit Valid Existing Vulnerability Data:

    • Priority: High

    • Details: Provide a complete and valid list of existing vulnerabilities (e.g., from penetration tests, vulnerability assessments, or previous audits) including their names, overviews, and severities. This will allow for a more holistic understanding of the organization’s current risk landscape.

General Security Enhancements

  1. Regular Review of Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Priority: Medium

    • Details: Periodically review the employee acceptable use policy to ensure it remains current with technological changes and evolving threat landscapes. Ensure the policy is clearly communicated and enforced.

  2. Enhance Ongoing Security Awareness Training:

    • Priority: Medium

    • Details: While annual training is conducted, consider augmenting it with more frequent, shorter modules, simulated phishing campaigns, and regular security bulletins to keep employees vigilant and informed throughout the year.
