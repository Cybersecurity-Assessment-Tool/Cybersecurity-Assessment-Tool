Executive Summary

The assessment revealed significant gaps primarily within organizational security controls and policies. Specifically, there is a lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) enforcement for email access and computer logins, which represents a critical vulnerability. Urgent recommendations include implementing comprehensive MFA, developing a robust acceptable use policy, and establishing mandatory security training for all new hires. Addressing these foundational elements will significantly improve the organization’s overall security posture.

Analysis of Security Control Gaps

The review of security controls reveals several critical areas needing immediate attention:

  • MFA Gaps: The lack of MFA for email access and computer logins poses a severe risk. Compromised credentials for either can lead to extensive breaches, data exfiltration, or ransomware attacks. While MFA for sensitive data systems is positive, the broader absence is a major concern.

  • Policy Deficiencies: The absence of an employee acceptable use policy means employees may not be aware of their responsibilities regarding company IT assets and data, increasing the risk of misuse or accidental data exposure.

  • New Employee Training Gap: While annual training for all employees is commendable, the lack of security awareness training for new employees leaves a significant vulnerability during their onboarding period, potentially exposing the organization to social engineering attacks or policy violations from the outset.

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and improve the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Action: Mandate and configure MFA for all organizational email accounts.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of email account compromise, even if passwords are stolen.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins:

    • Action: Deploy and enforce MFA for all employee computer logins, particularly for devices accessing the corporate network or sensitive data.

    • Benefit: Prevents unauthorized access to endpoints and internal resources, even if user credentials are compromised.

  3. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a clear, concise AUP outlining the proper and prohibited use of company IT resources, data, and internet access.

    • Action: Distribute the AUP to all employees and require acknowledgment of understanding and compliance.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear expectations for employee conduct, reduces misuse of resources, and mitigates legal and security liabilities.

  4. Establish Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Action: Integrate mandatory security awareness training into the onboarding process for all new hires.

    • Action: Cover topics such as phishing, password hygiene, data handling, and company policies.

    • Benefit: Ensures new employees are aware of security best practices and organizational policies from day one, reducing their susceptibility to common threats.

  5. Verify Network Scan Scope and Reachability (if applicable):

    • Action: Given no open ports were found, confirm that the scan correctly targeted the intended external perimeter and that firewall rules did not block the scan itself. If necessary, conduct an authenticated internal scan for a more comprehensive view.

    • Benefit: Ensures the technical assessment accurately reflects the organization’s exposure.

Conclusion

The assessment highlights that while no immediate external technical vulnerabilities were found, the organization faces significant risks due to deficiencies in fundamental security controls related to multi-factor authentication, employee policies, and initial security training. Prioritizing the implementation of the recommended actions will substantially strengthen the organization’s defense against common cyber threats and foster a more secure operational environment.
