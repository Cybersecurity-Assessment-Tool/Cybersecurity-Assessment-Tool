Risk Assessment

Based on the limited data available, the primary risks identified stem directly from the deficiencies highlighted in the security questionnaire.

Identified Risks

  1. Risk: Pervasive Credential Compromise due to Lack of MFA

    • Overview: The organization does not enforce Multi-Factor Authentication for critical access points including email, computer logins, and sensitive data systems.

    • Severity: Critical

    • Impact: High probability of unauthorized access through stolen or phished credentials. This can lead to email compromise, lateral movement within the network, data exfiltration, ransomware deployment, and severe business disruption.

  2. Risk: Increased Insider Threat and Policy Violation Exposure

    • Overview: The absence of an employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) means there are no formal guidelines for appropriate use of organizational IT resources.

    • Severity: High

    • Impact: Higher risk of accidental or malicious misuse of company assets, data leakage, introduction of malware, and non-compliance with industry regulations. Lack of clear policy makes disciplinary action difficult.

Recommendations

The following recommendations are critical to address the identified security gaps and enhance the overall security posture given the current information.

Immediate Actions

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Across All Critical Systems:

    • Action: Prioritize the deployment and enforcement of MFA for all user accounts accessing email (e.g., cloud productivity suites), logging into organizational computers/endpoints, and accessing sensitive data systems (e.g., CRM, ERP, HR systems, file shares). Extend MFA to VPN access.

    • Rationale: MFA significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access even if primary credentials are stolen, providing a robust defense against phishing and credential stuffing attacks.

  2. Develop and Implement a Comprehensive Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Draft, review, and formally implement a clear AUP that outlines acceptable and unacceptable use of company IT resources (internet, email, software, hardware). Ensure it covers data handling, privacy, and security expectations.

    • Action: Communicate the AUP to all employees, obtain signed acknowledgments, and integrate AUP review into annual security awareness training.

    • Rationale: An AUP establishes clear expectations for employee behavior, mitigates insider risks, supports legal and regulatory compliance, and provides a basis for enforcement.

Future Considerations (Contingent on Data Availability)

  1. Conduct a Comprehensive Network Vulnerability Scan:

    • Action: Once technical data input mechanisms are rectified, perform an in-depth vulnerability scan of both internal and external networks. This scan should identify open ports, insecure services, outdated software versions, and known vulnerabilities (CVEs).

    • Rationale: This will provide the critical technical insights missing from this assessment, allowing for proactive patching, hardening, and reducing the attack surface.

  2. Review and Prioritize Existing Vulnerabilities and Risks:

    • Action: Upon availability of existing vulnerability data, analyze and prioritize these findings based on severity, exploitability, and potential business impact. Develop a formal remediation plan with clear responsibilities, timelines, and regular progress tracking.

    • Rationale: A clear understanding and management of existing threats are essential for targeted risk reduction efforts and resource allocation.

Conclusion

This cybersecurity posture assessment, while constrained by the unavailability of critical network scan and existing vulnerability data, unequivocally highlights severe deficiencies in the organization’s foundational security controls. The pervasive lack of Multi-Factor Authentication and the absence of an Acceptable Use Policy represent immediate and critical risks that must be addressed without delay. The positive engagement in security awareness training provides a good base, but it cannot compensate for the lack of these essential technical and policy safeguards.
