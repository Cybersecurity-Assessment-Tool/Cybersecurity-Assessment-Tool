Analysis of Control Gaps

The questionnaire responses highlight significant deficiencies in fundamental cybersecurity hygiene:

  • MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: The lack of Multi-Factor Authentication for sensitive data systems is a critical vulnerability. This significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access should user credentials be compromised through phishing or other attacks.

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of a formal AUP means employees may not be aware of expected conduct regarding IT resource usage, potentially leading to unintentional policy violations, data leakage, or introduction of malware.

  • Security Awareness Training: No security awareness training for new employees or annual training for all employees is a severe weakness. The human element is often the weakest link in cybersecurity, and without regular training, employees are highly susceptible to social engineering attacks, phishing, and other common threats.

Recommendations

Based on the comprehensive assessment, the following actionable recommendations are provided to mitigate identified risks and enhance the organization’s cybersecurity posture.

Immediate Actions (High Priority)

  • Secure RDP Exposure:

    • Short-Term (Emergency): Implement strict firewall rules to restrict access to port 3389 (RDP) to only specific, trusted source IP addresses.

    • Long-Term (Best Practice): Place all RDP services behind a Virtual Private Network (VPN) or a secure gateway. Users must connect to the VPN first before RDP access is granted. Completely disable direct RDP exposure to the internet.

  • Implement MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Mandate and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication for all systems that process, store, or provide access to sensitive organizational data. This should be a top priority to protect against credential theft.

  • Initiate Comprehensive Security Awareness Training:

    • Develop and deliver mandatory security awareness training for all new employees as part of their onboarding process.

    • Schedule and enforce annual, recurring security awareness training for all existing employees. Training should cover current threat landscapes, including phishing, social engineering, ransomware, and safe browsing practices.

Medium-Term Actions

  • Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Create a clear, comprehensive Acceptable Use Policy outlining the proper and prohibited use of organizational IT resources, data, and systems. Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge this policy. Integrate AUP review into onboarding and annual employee performance cycles.

  • Regular Vulnerability Scanning and Penetration Testing: Implement a routine schedule for both internal and external vulnerability scans to proactively identify and address new security weaknesses as the environment evolves. Consider periodic penetration testing to simulate real-world attacks.

  • Develop and Test an Incident Response Plan (IRP): Create a formal Incident Response Plan that outlines procedures for detecting, analyzing, containing, eradicating, recovering from, and post-incident reviewing security incidents. Conduct tabletop exercises and drills to ensure the plan is effective and personnel are trained.
