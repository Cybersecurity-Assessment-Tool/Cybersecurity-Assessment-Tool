Analysis of Control Gaps

The "No" responses highlight the following critical deficiencies:

  • Inadequate MFA Implementation: Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is not required for logging into computers or accessing sensitive data systems. This significantly increases the risk of account compromise through phishing, brute-force attacks, or credential stuffing, as a single compromised password could grant an attacker access to critical internal resources.

  • Lack of Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of an AUP means employees may not be aware of their responsibilities regarding company IT assets and data, potentially leading to misuse, data breaches, or compliance violations.

  • Insufficient New Employee Security Training: Failing to provide security awareness training to new employees leaves them vulnerable to common social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing) from day one, posing an immediate risk to the organization.

Analysis of Technical Findings

The most significant technical finding is the open Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) service on port 3389. RDP, while necessary for remote administration, is a frequent target for attackers due to its common use and potential for vulnerabilities. Without proper security measures (such as VPN tunneling, strong passwords, and MFA), an open RDP port can lead to:

  • Brute-force Attacks: Attackers can attempt to guess login credentials.

  • Exploitation of RDP Vulnerabilities: Past vulnerabilities (e.g., BlueKeep) have allowed for remote code execution.

  • Initial Access Vector: A successful RDP compromise provides attackers with direct access to an internal system, facilitating lateral movement and further exploitation.

The scan data did not provide specific product or version information for the RDP service, preventing an assessment of known vulnerabilities specific to its version. However, the presence of an open RDP port alone constitutes a significant risk.

Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and improve the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided, prioritized by severity.

High and Critical Priority Recommendations

  1. Secure RDP Access:

    • Immediately restrict RDP access to trusted IP addresses only or ideally, place RDP behind a Virtual Private Network (VPN) gateway.

    • Implement strong, unique passwords for RDP accounts.

    • Enable Network Level Authentication (NLA) for RDP connections.

    • Consider implementing MFA for RDP access where direct exposure is unavoidable (though VPN is preferred).

    • Regularly patch and update the RDP service to protect against known vulnerabilities.

  2. Implement MFA for Computer Logins:

    • Deploy and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all computer logins across the organization. This significantly reduces the risk of successful attacks even if passwords are compromised.

  3. Implement MFA for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Enforce MFA for access to all systems containing sensitive organizational or client data. This adds a critical layer of security to prevent unauthorized data access.

  4. Establish New Employee Security Awareness Training:

    • Develop and implement a mandatory security awareness training program for all new employees during their onboarding process. This should cover common threats like phishing, social engineering, and safe internet practices.

Medium Priority Recommendations

  1. Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Create a clear and comprehensive AUP outlining employee responsibilities regarding the use of company IT resources, data handling, and internet usage.

    • Ensure all employees read, understand, and acknowledge the AUP, with regular reviews and updates.

Conclusion
