Risk Assessment

Based on the provided information, the primary risks identified stem from critical gaps in the organizational security posture, as revealed by the security questionnaire.

Identified Critical Gaps (from Questionnaire)

The "No" answers in the questionnaire represent high-severity risks due to their fundamental nature in protecting against common attack vectors.

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access: Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks, leading to potential business email compromise (BEC), data exfiltration, and lateral movement within the network.

  • Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Critical systems holding sensitive data are at severe risk if only relying on single-factor authentication. A compromised credential could grant an attacker direct access to confidential information.

  • Absence of an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Without an AUP, employees may engage in activities that inadvertently introduce risks, such as using unapproved software, visiting malicious websites, or mishandling company data, without clear guidelines or consequences.

  • No Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New employees are particularly vulnerable to social engineering and phishing attacks if not immediately trained on security best practices, making them potential entry points for attackers.

  • No Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees: Without regular training, employees may forget best practices, fail to recognize evolving threats, and become complacent, significantly increasing the organization’s human-factor risk.

Recommendations

To address the identified critical gaps and improve the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Enable and enforce MFA for all employee email accounts immediately. This is a foundational control against credential theft and phishing.

    • Educate users on the importance and usage of MFA.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Prioritize the implementation of MFA for all systems containing or accessing sensitive organizational data.

    • Review access controls to these systems to ensure least privilege is applied.

  3. Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Draft a clear and comprehensive AUP outlining acceptable and unacceptable use of company IT resources.

    • Ensure all employees read, understand, and sign the AUP upon hiring and periodically thereafter.

  4. Establish Mandatory Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Integrate security awareness training into the onboarding process for all new hires.

    • Cover topics such as phishing, social engineering, password hygiene, and data handling.

  5. Implement Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees:

    • Conduct mandatory, regular (at least annual) security awareness training for all employees.

    • Training should be engaging, up-to-date with current threats, and reinforced through simulated phishing exercises.

  6. Conduct Comprehensive Technical Scans:

    • Engage in regular (e.g., quarterly) external and internal network vulnerability scans.

    • Perform penetration testing at least annually to simulate real-world attacks.

    • Conduct web application vulnerability assessments for any public-facing applications.

  7. Review and Update Incident Response Plan:

    • Given the identified gaps, ensure the organization has a robust incident response plan that is tested periodically.

Addressing these recommendations will significantly improve [Organization Name]’s defense against a wide array of cyber threats and lay a stronger foundation for a resilient cybersecurity posture.
