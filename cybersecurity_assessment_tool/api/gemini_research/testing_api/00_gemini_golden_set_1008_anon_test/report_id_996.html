4. Risk Assessment

Based on the limited available data, the primary risks identified in this assessment stem from critical gaps in fundamental organizational security controls. The inability to conduct a technical scan or review existing vulnerabilities means that the full extent of technical risk remains unknown and unaddressed in this report.

4.1. Identified Gaps and Risks

  • CRITICAL: Lack of MFA for Email (SC-01)
    This is a severe vulnerability. Email accounts are a primary target for attackers, serving as a common vector for phishing, business email compromise (BEC), and initial access for lateral movement within a network. Without MFA, a simple password compromise can lead to widespread organizational impact.

  • CRITICAL: Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems (SC-03)
    Similar to email, systems containing sensitive organizational or client data are prime targets. The absence of MFA significantly elevates the risk of unauthorized access, data breaches, regulatory non-compliance, and severe reputational damage.

  • HIGH: Absence of Security Awareness Training (SC-05, SC-06)
    Human error remains a leading cause of security incidents. Without regular and mandatory security awareness training for both new hires and existing employees, the organization is highly susceptible to social engineering attacks (e.g., sophisticated phishing, vishing), malware infections, and unintentional policy violations. This represents a pervasive risk across the entire employee base.

4.2. Unknown Risks

The unavailability of network scan data and the existing vulnerability register means that specific technical risks, such as those related to outdated software, misconfigurations, or known Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVEs), cannot be identified or assessed at this time. This represents a significant and unquantified blind spot in the organization’s security posture.

5. Recommendations

To mitigate the identified risks and improve the overall security posture, the following actionable recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email and Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Immediate Priority: Roll out mandatory MFA for all corporate email accounts. This should be the highest priority for implementation.

    • Phased Rollout: Extend MFA implementation to all systems that store or process sensitive organizational or client data.

    • Strong MFA: Where feasible, consider implementing phishing-resistant MFA methods (e.g., FIDO2 security keys) to further enhance security.

  2. Establish and Maintain a Comprehensive Security Awareness Training Program:

    • Onboarding Training: Develop and implement a mandatory security awareness training program for all new employees during their onboarding process.

    • Annual Training: Implement mandatory annual (or more frequent) security awareness training for all employees. Topics should include, but not be limited to, phishing recognition, social engineering tactics, strong password hygiene, data handling best practices, and incident reporting procedures.

    • Reinforcement: Conduct regular simulated phishing exercises to test employee susceptibility and reinforce training concepts.

  3. Address Data Input Issues and Rerun Assessments:

    • Resolve Data Issues: Investigate and resolve the technical issues that led to the [BROKEN] network scan and vulnerability data inputs.

    • Perform Full Scan: Conduct a full, validated network scan against [Target IP] to accurately identify open ports, active services, running products, and their respective versions.

    • Vulnerability Management: Establish a robust vulnerability management program to maintain an accurate and up-to-date register of existing vulnerabilities, track their remediation, and ensure continuous monitoring.

    • Follow-up Assessment: Once accurate and complete data is available, perform a comprehensive technical analysis and update this report with specific findings, identified vulnerabilities, and tailored recommendations.
