Risk Assessment

Based on the available data, the primary risks identified stem from organizational policy and control deficiencies.

Identified Critical Gaps (from Security Control Review)

  • Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access: Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks. Compromised email can lead to business email compromise (BEC), data exfiltration, and further network penetration.

  • Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins: Similar to email, the absence of MFA for computer logins significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access to endpoints, potentially leading to privilege escalation and lateral movement within the network.

  • Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems: Critical systems containing sensitive organizational or client data are at severe risk without MFA. A single compromised password could expose proprietary information, intellectual property, or personally identifiable information (PII).

  • Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Without a formal AUP, employees may inadvertently or intentionally misuse company resources, introduce malware, or engage in activities that violate security protocols or legal requirements, leading to operational and legal risks.

Recommendations

The following recommendations are provided to address the identified critical gaps and improve the overall security posture:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) System-Wide:

    • Immediately implement MFA for all email accounts.

    • Deploy MFA for all computer login processes, especially for privileged users.

    • Enforce MFA for access to all systems containing sensitive data.

  2. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Draft a clear and concise AUP that outlines acceptable and unacceptable use of organizational IT resources, data, and internet access.

    • Ensure all employees review and acknowledge the AUP, preferably as part of onboarding and annual training.

  3. Conduct Comprehensive Technical Scans and Assessments:

    • Initiate a full network vulnerability scan to identify open ports, running services, software versions, and potential misconfigurations.

    • Perform regular penetration testing to simulate real-world attacks and uncover exploitable weaknesses.

    • Conduct web application security testing if the organization operates public-facing web applications.

  4. Establish a Robust Risk Management Framework:

    • Develop processes for identifying, assessing, treating, and monitoring risks continuously.

    • Maintain a comprehensive risk register to track all identified vulnerabilities, their severity, and remediation progress.

  5. Continue and Enhance Security Awareness Training:

    • Leverage existing security awareness training programs to educate employees on the importance of MFA, safe browsing habits, phishing recognition, and adherence to the new AUP.

    • Ensure training content is regularly updated to reflect current threat landscapes.
