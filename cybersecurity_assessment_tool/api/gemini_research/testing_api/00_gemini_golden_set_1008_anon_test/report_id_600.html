Identified Gaps from Questionnaire

The following high-priority risks and critical gaps were identified directly from the security control questionnaire:

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Logins: Exposes internal systems to credential-based attacks, allowing attackers easier lateral movement if initial access is gained.

  • Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Critical data is at higher risk of unauthorized access or exfiltration, potentially leading to significant financial and reputational damage.

  • Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Creates ambiguity regarding proper use of company resources, increasing legal and security risks due to inappropriate actions by employees.

  • No Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New hires are particularly susceptible to phishing and social engineering attacks, making them potential entry points for adversaries if not properly trained.

Recommendations

Based on the identified gaps and risks, the following recommendations are provided to enhance the cybersecurity posture of [Organization Name]. These actions are prioritized to address the most critical and high-risk findings.

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins:

    • Action: Mandate MFA for all user logins to company computers, laptops, and remote access solutions. Evaluate modern MFA solutions that integrate seamlessly with existing identity providers.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of successful credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks, thereby bolstering internal network security.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Action: Deploy MFA for access to all systems containing sensitive or critical organizational data, including databases, ERP/CRM systems, and cloud storage.

    • Benefit: Provides an additional, crucial layer of security for the most valuable assets, safeguarding against data breaches and ensuring compliance with data protection regulations.

  3. Develop and Enforce an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Action: Create a clear, comprehensive AUP that outlines acceptable and unacceptable use of company IT resources. Ensure all employees review, understand, and formally acknowledge the policy annually or upon updates.

    • Benefit: Establishes clear expectations for employee behavior, reduces insider risk, and provides a legal and procedural basis for addressing misuse of company assets.

  4. Establish Mandatory Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Action: Integrate comprehensive security awareness training into the onboarding process for all new hires. This training should cover topics such as phishing, social engineering, password hygiene, and the AUP.

    • Benefit: Equips new employees with essential knowledge to identify and avoid common cyber threats from day one, reducing their susceptibility to attacks and immediately fostering a security-conscious culture.

  5. Conduct a Comprehensive Technical Vulnerability Assessment:

    • Action: Address the issues preventing the parsing of technical scan data. Once valid data can be obtained, perform a full vulnerability assessment and penetration test of external and internal systems.

    • Benefit: Identifies exploitable weaknesses, misconfigurations, and outdated software versions that could be targeted by attackers, providing crucial insights for remediation.

  6. Maintain a Centralized Risk Register:

    • Action: Implement a process to systematically track, assess, and manage all identified cybersecurity risks, including those from technical scans, questionnaires, and other sources.

    • Benefit: Provides a holistic view of the organization’s risk posture, enables prioritized remediation efforts, supports informed decision-making, and demonstrates due diligence in risk management.
