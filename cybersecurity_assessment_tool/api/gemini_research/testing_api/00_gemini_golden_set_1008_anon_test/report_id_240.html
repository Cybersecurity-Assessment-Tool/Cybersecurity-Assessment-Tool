Analysis of Questionnaire Results

The organization has commendably implemented Multi-Factor Authentication across critical access points (email, computers, sensitive data systems), which significantly enhances protection against unauthorized access. This is a strong security practice.

However, two significant gaps were identified:

  • Lack of Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): The absence of an AUP leaves employees without clear guidelines on the appropriate use of organizational IT resources. This increases the risk of misuse, data breaches, and non-compliance with regulations. Without an AUP, the organization lacks a foundational document for enforcing security policies and managing employee behavior related to IT assets.

  • Missing Security Awareness Training for New Employees: While annual training for all employees is conducted, the lack of initial security awareness training for new hires means that new staff may operate without understanding fundamental security practices from day one. This oversight can lead to an increased risk of phishing attacks, social engineering, and unintentional security breaches during their initial, most vulnerable period of employment.

Recommendations

Based on the current assessment, the following actionable recommendations are provided to enhance the organization’s cybersecurity posture and address the identified gaps:

  1. Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Create a clear and comprehensive AUP outlining appropriate use of all company IT assets (networks, systems, data, devices, software).

    • Ensure all employees review and formally acknowledge their understanding of the AUP during onboarding and annually thereafter, or upon significant policy updates.

    • Integrate AUP principles into all employee handbooks and security awareness training programs.

  2. Implement Initial Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Develop a mandatory, robust security awareness training program specifically for new hires, to be completed within their first week of employment.

    • This training should cover essential cybersecurity topics such as identifying and reporting phishing attempts, strong password hygiene, proper data handling procedures, incident reporting protocols, and an overview of the AUP.

    • Supplement training with practical examples, quizzes, and interactive exercises to improve comprehension and retention.

  3. Conduct Regular and Comprehensive Technical Assessments:

    • Schedule regular (e.g., quarterly or semi-annually) external and internal vulnerability scans and penetration tests to identify technical weaknesses.

    • Ensure full and accurate data is available for these assessments to provide a complete and actionable picture of technical vulnerabilities across all systems.

    • Establish a robust vulnerability management program to prioritize and remediate identified vulnerabilities based on severity and potential impact.

  4. Maintain a Centralized Risk Register:

    • Establish and regularly update a centralized risk register to track, assess, and manage all identified cybersecurity risks, including those from technical scans, policy gaps, and external intelligence.

    • Assign clear risk owners, develop remediation plans with target dates, and monitor progress towards risk reduction.

  5. Continue Strong MFA Practices:

    • Commend the continued use and enforcement of Multi-Factor Authentication for email, computer login, and sensitive data systems.

    • Explore extending MFA to any other critical internal or external applications or services where it is not currently enforced, to further enhance access security.
