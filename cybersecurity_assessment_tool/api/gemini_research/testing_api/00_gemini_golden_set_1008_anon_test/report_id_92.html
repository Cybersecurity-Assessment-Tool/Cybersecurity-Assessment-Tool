Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive assessment of the cybersecurity posture for [Organization Name], based on an analysis of network scan data, responses to a security control questionnaire, and an existing risk register.

The assessment reveals several critical gaps, particularly concerning security policies and employee training. Key findings include the absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) and a complete lack of security awareness training for both new and existing employees. From a technical perspective, an open port 80 was identified on the target system, warranting further investigation to identify the service and ensure its secure configuration. An unusual entry in the existing risk register was also noted, highlighting the need for rigorous risk validation.

While the organization demonstrates strength in enforcing Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) across key access points, the identified policy, training, and technical vulnerabilities collectively present a significant attack surface and elevate the overall risk profile. Immediate action on the provided recommendations is advised to mitigate these risks and enhance the organization’s defensive capabilities.

Risk Assessment

This section consolidates risks identified from the questionnaire, technical scan, and pre-existing risk register entries.

Identified Critical Gaps from Questionnaire

The "No" responses from the security control questionnaire indicate critical gaps that significantly increase the organization’s risk exposure.

  • Lack of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Without a formal AUP, employees may be unaware of the expected behavior and responsible use of company IT assets. This lack of clear guidance can lead to unintentional misuse, data breaches, or compliance violations, posing a High Risk to data integrity and organizational security.

  • Absence of Security Awareness Training for New Employees: New hires are frequently targeted by social engineering attacks due to their unfamiliarity with organizational security protocols. The lack of initial security awareness training leaves new employees highly susceptible to phishing, malware, and other attacks, representing a Critical Gap that increases the likelihood of a successful breach.

  • Infrequent Annual Security Awareness Training: Security threats are constantly evolving. Without regular, at least annual, security awareness training for all employees, the workforce’s knowledge of current threats, best practices, and organizational policies will degrade over time. This significantly elevates the human-factor risk, making the organization more vulnerable to emerging threats and insider risks, posing a Critical Gap and a High Risk.

Technical Vulnerabilities

  • Open Port 80: The discovery of an open port 80 indicates an active service, typically a web server, is accessible from the network. While the scan data did not provide details on the specific service or its version, any internet-facing service is a potential attack vector. If this service is unpatched, misconfigured, or running outdated software, it could be exploited to gain unauthorized access, deploy malware, or deface websites. This represents a Medium Risk due to the potential for exploitation, which is elevated to High Risk without further information on the service’s security posture.

Recommendations

Based on the findings, the following recommendations are provided to enhance the cybersecurity posture of [Organization Name].

  1. Policy & Governance Enhancements:

    • Develop and Implement an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Immediately draft and implement a clear, comprehensive Acceptable Use Policy for all employees. This policy should outline responsibilities, permissible uses of IT resources, and consequences for non-compliance. Ensure all employees formally acknowledge and understand the AUP.

  2. Security Awareness Training Program:

    • Mandatory Onboarding Security Training: Establish a mandatory security awareness training program for all new employees, to be completed before they are granted full access to company systems. This training should cover phishing, malware, password best practices, and the new AUP.

    • Annual Refresher Training for All Employees: Implement a program of mandatory annual security awareness training for all employees. This training should be regularly updated to cover emerging threats, current social engineering techniques, and changes in organizational policies.

  3. Technical Security Controls:

    • Identify and Secure Services on Port 80:

      1. Immediately identify the exact service (e.g., Apache HTTP Server, Nginx) running on port 80 and its version.

      2. Ensure the service is strictly necessary and, if so, that it is fully patched to the latest stable version.

      3. Implement secure configuration best practices, disabling all non-essential features and modules.

      4. Consider restricting access to port 80 to only necessary internal or trusted external IP addresses, if feasible.

      5. Evaluate the implementation of a Web Application Firewall (WAF) to protect web services from common web-based attacks.

  4. Risk Management Process Improvement:

    • Review and Validate Risk Register Entries: Conduct a thorough audit of the organization’s existing risk register. All entries should be reviewed for accuracy, relevance, and actionability. Any anomalous or meta-instructional entries, like the one identified, should be clarified, removed, or properly categorized within a dedicated "testing" or "internal notes" section, distinct from active security risks. Ensure the risk management process includes regular review and validation cycles.
