5. Risk Assessment

This section details the identified risks, categorized by their source: organizational control gaps and technical findings.

5.1. Organizational Risks (from Security Control Review)

Based on the "No" responses in the security control questionnaire, the following critical organizational risks and control gaps were identified:

  • Risk 5.1.1: Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins

    • Severity: High

    • Overview: The absence of MFA for computer logins significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access to internal systems and data, even if user credentials are compromised.

  • Risk 5.1.2: Absence of an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

    • Severity: Medium

    • Overview: Without an AUP, employees may not be aware of their responsibilities regarding the use of company assets and data, leading to potential misuse, data breaches, or legal liabilities.

  • Risk 5.1.3: Lack of Security Awareness Training for New Employees

    • Severity: High

    • Overview: New employees are particularly vulnerable to social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing) and may unknowingly introduce security risks if not properly educated on organizational security policies and best practices from day one.

  • Risk 5.1.4: Lack of Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees

    • Severity: High

    • Overview: Without regular, annual security awareness training, employees’ knowledge of evolving threats and organizational policies can become outdated, diminishing their ability to recognize and respond to cyber threats effectively.

6. Recommendations

Based on the analysis, the following recommendations are provided to enhance the cybersecurity posture of [Organization Name]:

6.1. Address Organizational Control Gaps

  1. Implement MFA for Computer Logins: Mandate and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication for all computer login processes across the organization to significantly reduce the risk of unauthorized access due to compromised credentials.

  2. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy: Create a clear, comprehensive AUP that outlines employees’ responsibilities and acceptable behaviors regarding the use of company IT resources, data, and internet access. Ensure all employees acknowledge and understand this policy.

  3. Establish Security Awareness Training for New Employees: Integrate mandatory security awareness training into the onboarding process for all new hires. This training should cover phishing, malware, password hygiene, data handling, and relevant company policies.

  4. Implement Annual Security Awareness Training for All Employees: Conduct mandatory, regular (at least annual) security awareness training for all existing employees. Training content should be updated to reflect current threat landscapes and emerging best practices.

6.2. Technical Risk Management

  1. Verify and Reconcile "Unencrypted Web Server" Risk: Investigate the discrepancy between the pre-existing risk documentation (Port 80 open) and the current scan results (Port 80 closed).

    • Determine if the risk refers to a different system or IP address not covered by the current scan.

    • If the scanned target is indeed the system the risk refers to, and Port 80 is confirmed closed, then the risk should be formally closed and archived in the vulnerability management system.

    • If Port 80 is found to be open on a different system, ensure it is properly secured with HTTPS/TLS encryption.
