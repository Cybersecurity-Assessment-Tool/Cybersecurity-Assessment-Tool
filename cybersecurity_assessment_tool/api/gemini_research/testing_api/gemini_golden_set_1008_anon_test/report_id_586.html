5. Risk Assessment

Based on the analysis of the security questionnaire and the absence of other specific vulnerability data, the following key risks and control gaps have been identified:

Identified Risks and Control Gaps
Risk Name Severity Overview
Lack of MFA for Computer Login High The absence of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for computer logins significantly increases the risk of unauthorized access in the event of compromised credentials (e.g., via phishing or brute-force attacks). This is a critical control gap.
Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) High Without an AUP, employees lack clear guidelines regarding the appropriate use of organizational assets, data, and information systems. This can lead to increased legal, compliance, and security risks, including data breaches, malware infections, and misuse of resources.
Inadequate Security Awareness Training for New Employees Medium While annual security awareness training is conducted, the lack of initial training for new employees means they are not immediately equipped with essential security knowledge and best practices upon joining. This increases their susceptibility to social engineering attacks and potential policy violations during their initial employment period.

6. Recommendations

To address the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Logins:

    • Deploy and enforce MFA for all employee and administrative computer logins across the organization.

    • Evaluate various MFA solutions (e.g., authenticator apps, hardware tokens, biometrics) to select one that best fits the organization’s operational needs and security requirements.

    • Provide comprehensive training to employees on how to use MFA effectively.

  2. Develop and Implement an Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Draft a clear, concise, and comprehensive AUP that outlines acceptable and unacceptable use of company information systems, networks, data, and other IT resources.

    • Ensure the AUP covers topics such as data handling, internet usage, email etiquette, software installation, personal device usage (BYOD), and incident reporting.

    • Obtain formal acknowledgment from all employees that they have read, understood, and agree to adhere to the AUP. Integrate this into the onboarding process.

    • Periodically review and update the AUP to reflect changes in technology, threats, and organizational policies.

  3. Establish Mandatory Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Integrate a dedicated security awareness training module into the onboarding process for all new hires, to be completed before they gain access to company systems.

    • This initial training should cover fundamental security topics, including phishing recognition, password best practices, physical security, data classification, and the importance of the AUP.

    • Supplement this with regular refresher training as part of the annual security awareness program.

  4. Conduct a Comprehensive Technical Vulnerability Assessment:

    • Perform regular external and internal vulnerability scans and penetration tests on all internet-facing assets and internal networks.

    • This will identify technical vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, and outdated software versions that could be exploited by attackers, providing a more complete picture of the technical risk landscape.

7. Conclusion

While the organization has some security controls in place, the current assessment highlights critical gaps, particularly in foundational areas such as MFA for computer access, acceptable use policies, and initial security awareness training for new staff. Addressing these deficiencies proactively, in conjunction with future technical assessments, will significantly strengthen the organization’s defense against common cyber threats and enhance its overall resilience.
