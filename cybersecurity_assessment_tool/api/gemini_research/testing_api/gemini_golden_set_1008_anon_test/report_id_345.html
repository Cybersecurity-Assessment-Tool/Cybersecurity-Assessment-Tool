Analysis of Security Control Gaps

The review indicates several critical gaps:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access: Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to credential-based attacks, which can lead to broader organizational compromise.

  • Lack of MFA for Sensitive Data Systems: Critical systems housing sensitive data are vulnerable if protected by only a single factor, increasing the risk of data breaches.

  • Insufficient Annual Security Awareness Training: Employees are a primary target for cybercriminals. Lack of regular training can lead to decreased awareness of evolving threats and an increased likelihood of successful social engineering attacks.

Risk Assessment

This section correlates findings from the security control review, technical scan, and existing risk documentation to provide a consolidated risk assessment.

Consolidated Risk Assessment
Risk Name Overview Severity
Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access Organizations not enforcing MFA for email accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and account takeover. Email often serves as a primary vector for initial compromise and password reset mechanisms.
Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems Without MFA, access to sensitive data systems is protected only by a single factor, making them vulnerable to unauthorized access if credentials are compromised.
Insufficient Annual Security Awareness Training Regular and recurring security awareness training is crucial for keeping employees informed about evolving threats (e.g., phishing, social engineering) and maintaining a strong security culture. Lack of annual training increases the human attack surface.

Recommendations

To address the identified risks and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Enable and enforce MFA for all organizational email accounts. This significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access even if passwords are compromised.

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Sensitive Data Systems:

    • Mandate MFA for all access to systems handling sensitive organizational or client data.

  3. Establish and Enforce Annual Security Awareness Training:

    • Develop and implement a mandatory annual security awareness training program for all employees. Training should cover topics such as phishing, social engineering, password hygiene, and incident reporting.

  4. Investigate and Secure Port 8080:

    • Conduct an immediate investigation into the system listening on port 8080.

    • Verify the legitimacy of the "TOP SECRET DB" title and confirm if sensitive data is indeed exposed.

    • Re-evaluate the existing assessment that deems this port "secured" and a "false positive" in light of the information disclosure.

    • If the system is legitimate and required, ensure appropriate access controls, authentication, and encryption are in place. If not, the port should be closed or filtered.

    • Ensure no sensitive information is disclosed via HTTP titles or other readily accessible means.
