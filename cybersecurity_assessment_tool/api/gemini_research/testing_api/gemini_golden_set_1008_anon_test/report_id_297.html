Risk Assessment

This section synthesizes findings from the security control review, technical scan, and existing risk register to provide a holistic view of the organization’s risk posture. Risks are categorized and prioritized.

Identified Critical Gaps and High Risks

Based on the security control review, several critical gaps were identified:

  • Lack of MFA for Email Access (Critical): Without MFA, email accounts are highly susceptible to credential stuffing, phishing, and brute-force attacks. Compromised email can lead to significant data breaches and business email compromise (BEC) scams.

  • Lack of MFA for Computer Login (Critical): Similar to email, a lack of MFA for computer access increases the risk of unauthorized access to internal systems, data, and network resources if credentials are stolen or guessed.

  • No Security Awareness Training for New Employees (High): New employees are often the most vulnerable to social engineering attacks due to unfamiliarity with company policies and typical threats. This gap increases the risk of successful phishing, malware infection, and insider threats (accidental).

Technical Findings and Correlation

The Nmap scan revealed an open port 8080 with the HTTP title "TOP SECRET DB". While concerning on its face, the existing risk register (Input 3) explicitly labels "Port 8080 Secured" as a false positive with a CVSS score of 0.0. This indicates a pre-existing understanding and mitigation for this specific finding. However, it’s crucial to ensure this assessment remains valid through regular reviews.

Recommendations

To address the identified critical gaps and enhance the overall cybersecurity posture, the following recommendations are provided:

  1. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access:

    • Action: Mandate MFA for all user accounts accessing organizational email.

    • Benefit: Significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized email access due to compromised credentials, phishing, and credential stuffing attacks.

    • Priority: Critical

  2. Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Computer Login:

    • Action: Implement MFA for logging into all company-owned computers and workstations, especially those accessing sensitive data.

    • Benefit: Provides an additional layer of security, preventing unauthorized physical or remote access even if passwords are stolen or guessed.

    • Priority: Critical

  3. Establish Security Awareness Training for New Employees:

    • Action: Develop and deliver mandatory security awareness training for all new hires as part of their onboarding process.

    • Benefit: Educates new employees on common threats, company policies, and best practices, reducing human error vulnerabilities from day one.

    • Priority: High

  4. Regularly Review and Validate Open Port Statuses:

    • Action: Periodically re-evaluate the security posture of port 8080, especially given the "TOP SECRET DB" title. Confirm that the "secured/false positive" status remains accurate and that no new vulnerabilities have emerged due to configuration changes or new threat intelligence.

    • Benefit: Ensures that previously deemed secure services remain so, preventing complacency and detecting potential drift in configuration or new threats over time.

    • Priority: Medium (for re-validation)

Conclusion

The assessment reveals a mix of strong and weak security controls within [Organization Name]. While some foundational elements like an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) and annual security training are in place, critical deficiencies exist in MFA implementation for email and computer login, as well as in security awareness training for new employees. Addressing these gaps, particularly the MFA requirements, will significantly bolster the organization’s defense against common and impactful cyber threats. Continuous monitoring and validation of all services, including those deemed secure, are essential for maintaining a robust and adaptive security posture.
