3.1. Analysis of Questionnaire Findings

The questionnaire responses indicate several significant areas of concern that represent high risks to the organization:

  • Critical Gap: Lack of MFA for Email Access. The absence of MFA for email access is a severe vulnerability. Email is a primary vector for phishing, credential theft, and Business Email Compromise (BEC) attacks. Without MFA, a successful compromise of email credentials can quickly lead to unauthorized access to sensitive information, lateral movement within the network, and financial fraud.

  • High Risk: Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP). The lack of a formal AUP means employees may not be fully aware of their responsibilities regarding company IT resources, data handling procedures, and acceptable internet usage. This increases the risk of inadvertent or intentional misuse, data leakage, compliance violations, and legal liabilities.

  • High Risk: Lack of Comprehensive Security Awareness Training. The complete absence of security awareness training for both new and existing employees leaves the organization’s human element highly susceptible to social engineering attacks (e.g., sophisticated phishing, pretexting), malware infections, and human error-related incidents. Employees are often the primary target for attackers, and without proper training, they become the weakest link in the security chain.

5. Risk Assessment

This section summarizes the identified risks, primarily derived from the questionnaire analysis, with a note on the unavailability of other data sources.

5.1. Risks Identified from Questionnaire Analysis

  • Risk Name: Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Email Access

    • Overview: Without MFA on corporate email accounts, compromised credentials (e.g., obtained through phishing campaigns) can grant attackers full, unfettered access to internal communications, potentially leading to Business Email Compromise (BEC), sensitive data exfiltration, and further internal network penetration. This significantly elevates the risk of data breaches and financial losses.

    • Severity: Critical

  • Risk Name: Absence of Employee Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

    • Overview: Without a clear and enforced AUP, employees may inadvertently or intentionally misuse company IT resources, leading to data exposure, unauthorized software installations, non-compliance with regulations, and increased legal risks. This creates an environment where insecure practices can thrive.

    • Severity: High

  • Risk Name: Lack of Comprehensive Security Awareness Training

    • Overview: The complete absence of security awareness training leaves the entire workforce highly susceptible to social engineering attacks (e.g., phishing, pretexting, vishing), malware infections via user interaction, and other human-centric threats. This significantly increases the likelihood of successful security incidents, as employees are not equipped to recognize and defend against common attack vectors.

    • Severity: High

6. Recommendations

Based on the available information and identified gaps, the following actionable recommendations are provided to improve the cybersecurity posture of [Organization Name].

6.1. Immediate Actions (High Priority)

  1. Implement MFA for Email Access:

    • Immediately deploy and enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all corporate email accounts across the organization.

    • Prioritize strong MFA methods such as authenticator apps or hardware tokens over SMS-based MFA, if possible.

  2. Develop and Enforce an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP):

    • Create a comprehensive AUP that clearly outlines employee responsibilities regarding company assets, data handling, internet usage, and prohibited activities.

    • Ensure all employees read, understand, and formally acknowledge this policy upon hiring and periodically thereafter.

  3. Initiate a Security Awareness Training Program:

    • Implement mandatory security awareness training for all new hires as part of their onboarding process.

    • Establish a recurring annual security awareness training program for all existing employees to keep them informed about current threats and best practices.

    • Focus initial training modules on critical topics such as identifying phishing emails, strong password policies, data handling best practices, and reporting suspicious activities.

6.2. Further Assessment and Strategic Actions (Medium Priority)

  1. Conduct a Comprehensive External Network Scan: Engage with a qualified cybersecurity firm to perform a thorough external network vulnerability scan and penetration test. This will identify open ports, exposed services, outdated software versions, and potential misconfigurations that could not be determined in this report.

  2. Review and Document Internal Vulnerabilities & Risk Register: Establish or review a formal process to continuously track, assess, and manage internal vulnerabilities and risks. If an existing risk register is available, ensure it is up-to-date, actively maintained, and used for prioritized remediation planning.

  3. Develop and Test an Incident Response Plan (IRP): Create a detailed Incident Response Plan that outlines procedures for detecting, analyzing, containing, eradicating, recovering from, and post-incident reviewing security incidents. Conduct regular tabletop exercises to test the IRP’s effectiveness and train key personnel.
